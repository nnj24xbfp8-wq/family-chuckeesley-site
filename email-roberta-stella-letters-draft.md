# Email to Roberta — the Stella mystery and the 1940s letters

**Subject:** A question for the 1940s Charles Leonard letters — the Stella mystery

---

Dear Roberta,

I've been working through the family archive with my dad's letters and Grandma Peggy's collections, and one piece keeps coming back as the central unsolved mystery: **how Stella came to live with the Eesleys.**

Your 2019 note remains the closest anyone in the family has come to a real reconstruction:

> *"Stella is a mystery to me. No one is alive who remembers exactly how she became part of the family. She may have joined the scene after Lyle died ... Mom said Stella lived with the Eesleys in the family home in Bexley (they often took in boarders) until sometime in the 50s. When Stella left, all the other Eesley kids had grown and gone, and Grandpa fell into a deep depression."*

The dates line up. Stella was born **5 October 1925 in Honolulu**, daughter of Koon Hung Sunn and Mabel Lee Sunn — both Chinese-American Hawaiians. By the time she arrived in Bexley around 1942-43 she was 17 or 18. Lyle died at Cabanatuan POW camp in the Philippines in **July 1942**.

The family-memory line that has come down through Aunt Jeanne is that **Stella came on a boat from Manila** — the Philippines was a waypoint, not her origin. The reading I've worked out (with help from the historical record) is that she was probably visiting family or attending school in Manila in 1941, was caught by the Japanese invasion in December 1941, spent some portion of 1942 at the **Santo Tomas Internment Camp** in Manila (the main civilian internment site for American citizens caught in the Philippines), and was repatriated to New York on one of the two **MS Gripsholm diplomatic-exchange voyages** in 1942 or 1943.

From New York, she was placed somehow with the Eesleys in Bexley — possibly through a church-based refugee-resettlement network, possibly through a personal contact made via Lyle's POW circle, possibly through some other channel I haven't thought of.

That last leg — **the actual mechanism by which Stella's name reached Grandpa Charles** — is the open question.

**The letters you have are almost certainly the place the answer sits.** You mentioned a "huge collection of mostly 1940s letters" in Grandpa's "almost indecipherable" handwriting. If you have a chance to look through them with this specific question in mind, I'd love to know:

1. Do any of the 1940s letters mention **a placement agency, a refugee-resettlement organization, the Red Cross, a church-based program**, or any name that reads as a relief-agency name?
2. Is there any reference to **Manila, the Philippines (other than Lyle), Santo Tomas, the Gripsholm, an exchange voyage**, or an American civilian who had been in the Pacific?
3. Does anyone he was corresponding with in 1942-43 sound like a possible connection — a fellow Bexley family with a Pacific connection, someone he met through Lyle's POW circle, a minister or priest?
4. Is there a first letter or thank-you-note from or about Stella — anything that sounds like it might be her first contact with the family, or the family's first reference to her?

Any one of those would close the loop on a fifty-year family mystery.

I know the handwriting is hard. Even partial transcription, or just photographs of the most promising-looking pages, would be a huge gift. If you're game for it, I'd be happy to help — I could come visit and look through them with you in person, or I could pay for digitization (photographing or scanning the whole collection) so we have a working text version that future generations can read.

Either way — no pressure. The mystery has waited fifty years. It can wait a little longer.

Thank you for everything you've already shared — the c. 1973-75 reunion labels, the Joseph Hill Chenoweth and John K. Timmons photographs, the Mary Bean material. The archive is so much richer for it.

With love,

Chuck
