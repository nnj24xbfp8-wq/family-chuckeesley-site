The documented record (FamilySearch ID **L614-Z2P**, seven sources): **Stella Elaine Sunn**, born October 5, 1925, Honolulu, Oahu, Territory of Hawaii. Her parents were **Koon Hung Sunn** (b. ~1898) and **Mabel Lee Sunn** (b. ~1900) — Chinese-American, Hawaii-resident. In 1940 the family was living in Representative District 5, Oahu. She died **February 15, 1971**, age 45, and is buried in First Presbyterian Church Cemetery in Moorestown Township, Burlington County, New Jersey — the same town in which her husband Ted would later die and is presumably also buried.

The family memory (Aunt Jeanne's account, kept here in her words because the act it records is the reason this entry exists):

> *"Stella was a girl from Hawaii and I think she might have been from a mixed parentage (Chinese & Japanese). At any rate she was in danger of being incarcerated in the concentration camps in the west during World War II. So grandpa Eesley brought her over to live with them during the war and she became part of the family at that time. She later married to Dr. Ted Chong and they had two children — SueLynn and I forget what the boy's name was. But they used to send us macadamia nuts and toasted coconut cans from Hawaii before they were ever popular here and it was so fun to get those packages. They later moved to Philadelphia and were divorced."*

**A correction the records permit.** Stella's parents were both Chinese-American by surname and by Honolulu residence; she was not, so far as the documentary record shows, of Japanese descent. The wartime exposure Jeanne remembered may have been the broader pattern under which East Asian appearance — without distinction between Chinese, Japanese, Korean, or mixed — was treated with suspicion across the West Coast. Whatever the precise mechanism, the act it provoked — [Charles Leonard Eesley](/family/charles-leonard-eesley/) bringing her across the country into his house — was the same.

She was sixteen when the United States entered the war, twenty when it ended, and forty-five when she died. Aunt Jeanne also recovers, by way of Ted's obituary, the name of the son whose name she had forgotten: **[Glenn](/family/glenn-chong/)**. Her daughter Sue Lin appears in the documentary spelling on her father's obituary and as **SueLynn** in family memory; both are kept on her [page](/family/suelynn-chong/).

She appears in the [late-1940s/'60s family group portrait](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-late-1940s/) standing beside Ted, with Uncle Will in front. The photograph and her early death in 1971 bracket roughly twenty-five years of her adult life inside this family.

> *Sources: [FamilySearch L614-Z2P](https://www.familysearch.org/en/tree/person/L614-Z2P) (seven sources, one memory); Ted Chong's published obituary (Burlington County Times, 2013); Aunt Jeanne's oral account, May 2026.*
