She was the **second of the ten Albert Robert Sr Eesley children**, born in Canada during the family's Ontario years — at **Ayr** or **Hamilton**, where her father had come to work as a miller for the Goldie family and where her mother [Jennie Goldie](/family/jeanie-goldie/) had grown up. Per [William Eesley's c. 1916 obituary](/docs/william-eesley-obituary-college-corner/), she and her older brother [John R.](/family/john-r-eesley/) were the only two of the ten children born before their father started moving the family around Michigan to build and operate mills:

> *They were married there [in Ayr, Canada] and two children were born to them, J. R., who follows milling, and Mrs. J. B. Scoon, of Toronto.*

Her **given name was Hanna** per the family's *Eesleys circa 1900* PowerPoint caption — the spelling is what appears in the typed family caption. **"Anna"** was the everyday nickname, and the form in which her name reaches the present day.

## The Toronto life

She married **J. B. Scoon** and made her home in **Toronto** for the rest of her life. By the time of William's c. 1916 obituary she was one of **four Eesley daughters living in Toronto**: she as Mrs. Scoon, her sister Jennie as Mrs. Baxter, a third sister as Mrs. George Sinclair, plus the bachelor brother [Dr. George the physician](/family/george-eesley-toronto/). Their parents Albert Robert Sr and Jennie Goldie also retired and died in Toronto. The Toronto Eesleys were a substantial late-Victorian extended family — sons-in-law, daughters-in-law, the next generation of cousins coming up around them.

Toronto in the 1880s and 1890s — when Hanna would have married — was a city growing rapidly from a colonial Loyalist town into a North American commercial hub. The street grid of what is now downtown Toronto was being filled in. **St. James's Cathedral, Yonge Street, Queen's Park, the early streetcar lines** all date from her young-adult years there. The British loyalty of the city's institutions was strong; the Scottish-Presbyterian Protestant culture her mother's family had brought from Ayrshire was a dominant strand of Toronto's middle-class life. A married Scoon woman in Toronto in the 1890s would likely have been part of that culture: a Presbyterian church congregation (the Knox or St. Andrew's pews), the women's social and missionary societies, the daily round of housekeeping in a brick downtown house with a small yard and a coal furnace.

J. B. Scoon's full name and his trade are not yet documented in this archive — Toronto city directories of the 1890s and 1900s, available through the Toronto Public Library's archive, would settle both. The Scoons may have had children; if so they are Chuck's first cousins three times removed and represent a Canadian Eesley branch parallel to the American Charles Leonard line.

She appears in the **[1899 Eesley family group portrait](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-1899/)** as "Anna" in the seated row — by then a married Toronto woman in her thirties or forties, posed with her parents and siblings in the studio at the family's reunion-portrait moment.
