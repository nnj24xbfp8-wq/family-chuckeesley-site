She was the **second of the ten Albert Robert Sr Eesley children** — born in **Paris, Ontario, on 20 December 1863** during the family's Canadian years, with [John Franklin](/family/john-f-eesley/) ahead of her by four years. Her **given name was Hannah Goldie** per Dale Eesley's FamilySearch tree (ID **LBKQ-8R3**), the middle name honoring her **Scottish-born mother [Jennie Goldie](/family/jeanie-goldie/)** of Ayrshire. **"Anna"** was the everyday nickname — and the form in which her name reaches the present day.

Per [William Eesley's c. 1916 obituary](/docs/william-eesley-obituary-college-corner/), she and her older brother [John Franklin](/family/john-f-eesley/) were the first two of the ten children born before their father started moving the family around Michigan to build and operate mills.

## The Toronto life

She married **J. B. Scoon** and made her home in **Toronto** for the rest of her life. By the time of William's c. 1916 obituary she was one of **four Eesley sisters in Toronto** — she as Mrs. Scoon, her sister [Jennie](/family/jennie-baxter-eesley/) as Mrs. Baxter, her sister [Bessie](/family/bessie-eesley/) as Mrs. Sinclair, plus their bachelor brother [Dr. George Henry Eesley the physician](/family/george-eesley-toronto/). Their parents [Albert Robert Sr](/family/albert-robert-eesley/) and [Jennie Goldie](/family/jeanie-goldie/) had also retired and died in Toronto.

Toronto in the 1880s and 1890s — when Hannah would have married — was a city growing rapidly from a colonial Loyalist town into a North American commercial hub. The street grid of what is now downtown Toronto was being filled in. **St. James's Cathedral, Yonge Street, Queen's Park, the early streetcar lines** all date from her young-adult years there. The British loyalty of the city's institutions was strong; the Scottish-Presbyterian Protestant culture her mother's family had brought from Ayrshire was a dominant strand of Toronto's middle-class life. A married Scoon woman in Toronto in the 1890s would likely have been part of that culture: a Presbyterian church congregation, the women's social and missionary societies, the daily round of housekeeping in a brick downtown house with a small yard and a coal furnace.

She died on **20 June 1932** at age 68 — six months before her younger sister Jennie Baxter (d. 10 January 1935) and four months after their brother [William](/family/william-eesley-college-corner/) the College Corner miller (d. 24 April 1932). The early 1930s took three of the ten siblings within three years of each other. She was buried at **Pine Hills Cemetery, Scarborough**, in the eastern part of Toronto.

She appears in the **[1899 Eesley family group portrait](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-1899/)** as "Anna" in the seated row — by then a married Toronto woman of about 36, posed with her parents and siblings at the family's reunion-portrait moment.
