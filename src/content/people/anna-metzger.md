She was born in **1650** in the Württemberg countryside — the same year as her future husband, in the same fragile post-war landscape, in a village rebuilding from the **Thirty Years' War** that had just ended two years earlier. The world of her childhood was the slow recovery: vineyards being replanted, parish registers being reconstructed, the village population growing back from a generation of catastrophe. She married [Johannes Wildermuth](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1650/) somewhere around her early twenties; their son [Johannes Jr.](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1682/) was born in **1682**, when she was thirty-two.

## Forty-four years a widow

Her husband died in **1694**. She was **forty-four**. She lived another **thirty-four years**, dying in **1728** at age **seventy-eight**.

What that long widowhood would have looked like is a piece of late-17th and early-18th-century Württemberg rural life worth pausing on. A widow in this society had **definite legal standing** under Württemberg customary law: she retained her own dowry property, she could manage the family holding on behalf of her children, she could enter into contracts and run a household head-of-household. Many widows did not remarry — the unmarried widow at the head of a household was a common figure of the village. She would have continued to work whatever the family's plot produced (vineyard rows if Wildermuth family land was already in vines by 1694, a garden plot, perhaps a milk cow, some chickens), and she would have done it with her son's growing help.

Her son Johannes Jr. would have been **twelve** at his father's death, ready to begin taking on the man's share of the work. By the time he married and started his own family in the 1710s, Anna would have moved into the **grandmother's place** of the village rural household — the elder woman with the loom or the knitting needles by the fire, the keeper of the family stories, the one who remembered the old language and the old customs that the younger generation was beginning to forget.

She lived long enough to see her grandson **[Johann Adam](/family/johann-adam-wildermuth-1720/)** born in **1720** — Chuck's six-times-great-grandfather, who would die in 1768 the same year his own grandson Adam was born. Whether Anna lived long enough to know that her grandson Johann Adam was raising his family does not survive in the documentary record. She died in **1728**, when Johann Adam was eight — old enough to have memories of his grandmother's face, the old woman in the cottage who had been a Württemberg girl in 1650.
