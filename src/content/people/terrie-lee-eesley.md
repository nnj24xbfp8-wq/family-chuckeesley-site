Terrie Lee was born **January 13, 1949 in Oakland, California**, to [Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/) and [Dorothy Marie Davis Wildermuth](/family/dorothy-davis-wildermuth/). The Oakland birth is the often-overlooked timing detail: her father was finishing his Stanford B.A. in Biological Sciences in October 1948 and was still in the Bay Area when she was born three months later. She is, in that quiet sense, a Stanford-era child &mdash; the first of the family to be born in California, three decades before her son Chuck would join the Stanford faculty.

## The memorial speech

The fullest portrait of her, by far, is the speech Chuck delivered at her memorial service on 8 July 2017 &mdash; the companion piece to [his eulogy for his father](/docs/eulogy-charles-mcmaster-eesley/), and the second piece of original work in his own voice in this archive:

**[Read "Memorial Speech for Terrie Lee Bain" (by Chuck Eesley, 2017)](/docs/memorial-speech-terrie-lee-bain/)**

A few of the things that page carries that the obituary cannot: her late-night studying after long days teaching and cooking dinner; her mailing the Duke acceptance back herself, then telling Chuck *"don't worry, I will find a way"*; the giant stuffed Easter bunnies; the rule that her father moved around so much for the Air Force that **she wanted to stay in one place so Chuck would have a set of childhood friends**; her saying *life wasn't meant to live alone, life was meant to be shared with another;* and Chuck's three closing promises.

The fullest portrait of her in her *own* voice is the eight-line poem she wrote and that was read at the service:

**[Read "Please don't cry for me" — a poem by Terrie Lee Bain](/docs/please-dont-cry-for-me-terrie-bain/)**

*"We traveled the country for Uncle Sam / Til settling in Ohio for a life with Fam. / I had a son and 3 siblings to love / My books and students, so why move?"* The whole biography is in those four lines.

The teachers and students of **Harmar Elementary School**, where Terrie had been the Title I teacher in her last years, wrote a children's tribute, framed around the butterfly effect. It was first read at the school's end-of-year assembly that May &mdash; coordinated by [Cheryl Cook](/family/cheryl-cook/), with the choir singing *"I Believe,"* a song Terrie had liked through the wall from her classroom, and the four staff members who worked closest with her &mdash; **Ellen, Jean, Sandy, Anne** &mdash; releasing balloons as the song ended. Chuck then asked his aunts whether it belonged in the memorial program, and read it aloud at the service in July:

**[Read "Mrs. Bain and the Butterfly Effect" — Tribute from Harmar School (May 2017)](/docs/mrs-bain-and-the-butterfly-effect/)**

*"Mrs. Bain is not famous like Dr. Seuss and J.K. Rowling, but what she said and did has already had important results."*

## The biographical record

She grew up an Air Force kid &mdash; *"Terrie traveled the U.S. and Japan until graduating high school,"* her obituary's compact line for the postings that took her father from the Pacific war's aftermath through Korea, the Pentagon, and the rest of the career. **Japan was part of her childhood, not just Robert Earl's.**

She came back to Ohio for college, taking her degrees at **Marietta College** and the **University of Maryland** &mdash; a Bachelor's and a Master's in early childhood education, plus a string of later certifications, the last a one-year **Master Teaching Certification** program. She spent **twenty-three years** in the Marietta City School District. The obituary names the specific posts: **children's librarian at the Marietta Public Library**, **teacher at North Hills Elementary**, and **Title I teacher at Harmar Elementary**. In the summers she taught **Safety Town**.

She married [Charles M. Eesley](/family/charles-eesley/) in the 1970s and they raised Chuck on Highland Ridge Road. After that marriage ended she married, fifteen years before her death, **[Benjamin F. Bain II](/family/ben-bain/)** &mdash; he predeceased her by two and a half years (d. 21 December 2014). Chuck's father followed eleven months after that, in November 2015. Terrie died at home in Marietta on **16 May 2017**, age 68. **The same age her former husband had been at his death** &mdash; both of Chuck's parents died at 68.

## The portrait

The photograph at the head of this page shows Terrie at the **Stanford Alumni Center**, looking through her father Robert Earl Wildermuth's **Class of '48 yearbook** — the volume that holds [his graduation from Stanford on the GI Bill](/archive/robert-earl-wildermuth-stanford-ba-1948/). It is the family's quietest single statement of why this campus matters to it: the daughter on the same campus, with her father's record open in front of her, in the same building her son had become a faculty member of. The Stanford thread runs through her too &mdash; not as a graduate, but as the keeper of her father's record on the campus where her son now works. Three generations meeting across an open yearbook page.

## The memorial service

A memorial service was held **July 8, 2017 at 2:00 p.m.** at the Hadley Funeral Reno Chapel, 1021 Pike Street, Marietta. Cremation. The printed program shows speakers in order: **Chuck Eesley, son; Sandy Clement, sister; Debbie Massaro, sister; [Daniel Ebaugh](/family/daniel-ebaugh/), friend; Tribute from the Teachers and Students of Harmar School (read by Chuck Eesley).** Terrie's own poem was printed alongside the obituary.
