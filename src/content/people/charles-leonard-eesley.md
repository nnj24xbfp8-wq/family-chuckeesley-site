Charles Leonard Eesley is the bridge generation in two senses. Biographically he is the child of the emigrant Albert and his Scottish wife Jeanie Goldie, and the husband of Lillie Dale Chenoweth who carried the Chenoweth and Timmons lines into the family. But the act that gives him his own place in the story is from the next generation's wartime.

During the Second World War he brought **[Stella](/family/stella/)**, a young woman from Hawaii at risk of being incarcerated in the West Coast Japanese-American internment camps, into his household — Aunt Jeanne's account, simply: *"grandpa Eesley brought her over to live with them during the war and she became part of the family at that time."* She remained part of the family for the rest of her life, married Dr. Ted Chong, raised two children, sent macadamia nuts back to Ohio. That she is in the [late-1940s family group portrait](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-late-1940s/) — years after the immediate wartime emergency had passed — is the proof that the shelter was not a temporary measure.
