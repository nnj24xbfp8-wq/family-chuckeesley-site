**Jack Ridenour** married **[Ruth Irene Wildermuth](/family/ruth-irene-wildermuth-ridenour/)** &mdash; Robert Earl Wildermuth's older sister &mdash; on **6 May 1943 at Camp Kilmer, New Jersey**. Camp Kilmer was the U.S. Army's main East-Coast staging area for soldiers shipping to the European and CBI theaters of World War II; **Jack and Ruth had one week of marriage before Jack shipped out**.

## The China-India-Burma Theater

Jack served in the **China-India-Burma Theater** (CBI) &mdash; the Pacific war's western flank, running supply over the Hump from India into China, and supporting British and Commonwealth ground forces against the Japanese in Burma. The CBI was the **least-known of the four major American theaters of World War II** &mdash; smaller-scale and lower-profile than Europe, the Pacific Islands, or the European-Mediterranean theaters &mdash; but materially essential to keeping China in the war. Jack's specific unit, rank, and assignment are open research.

While Jack was overseas, Ruth went back to Marietta and lived as a **"warbride widow"** until his return at the end of the war. After their reunion they raised two daughters in the postwar years:

- **Karen Kay Ridenour**, born 8 January 1949
- **Marcia Ann Ridenour**, born 7 April 1951

## The multiple myeloma cluster

Per Ruth's autobiographical sketch in [Robert Earl's 1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/), Jack **later died of multiple myeloma** &mdash; the same blood cancer that had killed his mother-in-law **[Sadye Irene (Fleming) Wildermuth](/family/sadye-fleming-wildermuth/)** in August 1976. This is an **unusual cluster of the same uncommon cancer** within one extended family across one generation; whether environmental, coincidental, or carrying any genetic component is open. The pattern is part of the medical history Robert Earl's memoir flags for *"any of my progeny that I may have passed the diabetic gene onto, have faith."*

His exact dates of birth and death are open research.

> *Sources: Ruth Irene Wildermuth Ridenour's parallel autobiographical sketch, in [Robert Earl Wildermuth's 1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/).*
