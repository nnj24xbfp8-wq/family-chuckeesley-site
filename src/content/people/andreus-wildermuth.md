Andreus Wildermuth was born around **1748** in **[Rielingshausen](/places/rielingshausen-church/)**, the small village in the **Württemberg** region of southwestern Germany, about 20 kilometers northeast of Stuttgart. He was the son of **[Johann Adam Wildermuth](/family/johann-adam-wildermuth-1720/)** (1720&ndash;1768) and **[Rosina Kreher](/family/rosina-kreher/)** (b. 1721) per the FamilySearch family tree that Chuck transmitted in June 2026.

He was the **deepest documented Wildermuth ancestor in this archive at the time of [Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/)'s 1990 research** &mdash; reported in his [1990 Wildermuth/Fleming Heritage](/docs/wildermuth-fleming-heritage-1990/). The FamilySearch tree later extended the line three more generations back through Johann Adam to [Johannes Wildermuth](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1650/) (b. 1650).

## Occupation: Gartner (vineyard gardener)

Robert Earl recorded his occupation as ***Gartner* — vineyard gardener** &mdash; the trade of cultivating and tending the vineyards that produced the wines of the Neckar river valley. The Württemberg Neckar wine country was already a centuries-old vinicultural region by Andreus's lifetime; the small Rielingshausen vineyards around the Marbach area are still in cultivation today. To be a *Gartner* in Rielingshausen in the late 18th century was to belong to the village's agricultural craft layer &mdash; tenant or smallholder vineyard work, the steady trade of every wine-producing village in southwest Germany.

## His son

The only documented child in this archive is **[Adam Wildermuth](/family/adam-wildermuth/)** (b. 1768 Rielingshausen), who followed his father into the *Gartner* trade in the same village.

## What Robert Earl could not trace — and what the FamilySearch tree later did

Robert Earl's 1990 research notes the limit of what he could trace:

> *I have not attempted to go back any farther than Andreus &mdash; my goal at the start was to find the first Wildermuth in America. My research about Andreus is informational only; nothing more.... I don't know who his father was or his mother. Andreus, our fourth great-grandfather. I do know that he was the father of Adam Wildermuth, and that he was a gartner (vineyard gardener) in Rielingshausen.*

In June 2026, Chuck transmitted a FamilySearch family-tree screenshot that **answers Robert Earl's open question**. Andreus's parents were **Johann Adam Wildermuth (1720&ndash;1768)** and **Rosina Kreher (b. 1721)**. The line continues backward to Johannes Wildermuth (1682), to Margaretha Grayers (1685), and to **Johannes Wildermuth (1650&ndash;1694)** and Anna Metzger (1650&ndash;1728) — the deepest documented Wildermuth ancestors anywhere in this archive, **two years after the Treaty of Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War.**

## The two Adams

A naming pattern worth noting: **Andreus's father, Johann Adam, died in 1768. Andreus's son was named Adam, and was born in 1768.** The son was named after the just-deceased grandfather. The two Adams in adjacent generations — one dying, one born, the same year — produced a surface confusion that earlier research (including Robert Earl's) navigated without surfacing. Both are real; both are documented.

He is **Chuck's five-times-great-grandfather** through the line:

> Andreus &rarr; [Adam Wildermuth](/family/adam-wildermuth/) (b. 1768) &rarr; *unconfirmed intermediate* &rarr; **[Johann Michael Wildermuth](/family/johann-michael-wildermuth/)** (b. ~1831 Grossaspach, the 1847 emigrant to America) &rarr; *unconfirmed intermediate* &rarr; **John Wildermuth** &rarr; **[Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/)** (Robert Earl's father) &rarr; **[Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/)** &rarr; **[Terrie Lee Wildermuth Eesley](/family/terrie-lee-eesley/)** &rarr; Chuck.

The two intermediate generations between Adam and Johann Michael, and between Johann Michael and Earl Adam's father, are queued for research follow-up against the LDS International Genealogical Index records that Robert Earl consulted.
