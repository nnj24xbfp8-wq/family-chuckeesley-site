He was born around **1748** in **[Rielingshausen](/places/rielingshausen-church/)** — the small Württemberg village 20 kilometers northeast of Stuttgart, on the Neckar river, in the heart of the German wine country. His parents were [Johann Adam Wildermuth](/family/johann-adam-wildermuth-1720/) (1720&ndash;1768) and [Rosina Kreher](/family/rosina-kreher/) (b. 1721). When his father died — in the same year Andreus's own son Adam was born — Andreus was **twenty**, and the timing of those two events (a father's death, a son's birth in his name) is the kind of compressed family moment that small Württemberg villages must have known a hundred ways.

## A *Gartner* in the Neckar vineyards

His trade was **Gartner** — German for *gardener*, but in the Württemberg countryside of the 18th century, specifically a **vineyard gardener / cultivator**, tending the steep south-facing slopes along the Neckar and its tributary streams. The wine country between Marbach, Backnang, and Rielingshausen had been planted in grapes since the medieval period; the **Trollinger** and **Riesling** vines his hands managed grow there to this day. A *Gartner* was the village's specialist in the long, slow seasonal arc of viticulture: pruning the vines in March, training the new shoots in May, thinning the canopy in July, watching for mildew through the wet weeks of August, and bringing in the harvest in late September or October. The work was done largely by hand, with simple tools — pruning knives, hoes, baskets — and the long days were structured by the small village's bells and the position of the sun on the slopes.

The wine the vines produced was almost entirely for **local consumption**. Württemberg's wine industry would not become widely export-oriented until the 19th century. Andreus's work fed the local taverns, the church communion table, the household tables of village families who drank their wine from earthenware mugs and the cellar windows of the merchant houses in nearby Marbach. Whatever Andreus knew about the work — how to read the soil, how to time the harvest by the changing weight of the grapes in his hands — he had learned from his father Johann Adam, who had learned it from *his* father, and so back to **[Johannes Wildermuth](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1650/)** in the year of the Peace of Westphalia.

## What he passed on

He married, fathered children, and lived out a *Gartner*'s life in the village. The only child documented in this archive is **[Adam Wildermuth](/family/adam-wildermuth/)**, born **1768** — the year Andreus's own father Johann Adam died, and the year Andreus named his son after the just-deceased grandfather. Adam followed Andreus into the same trade. Somewhere down that line, a generation or two further (the intermediate records have not yet surfaced), came **[Johann Michael Wildermuth](/family/johann-michael-wildermuth/)** — the great-great-grandson who would, in **1847**, do what no Wildermuth had done in nearly two hundred years: **leave Württemberg.**

Robert Earl Wildermuth, doing the genealogical research in Orlando in the 1980s, identified Andreus as the deepest ancestor he could reach. His 1990 note, the closing summary of what he had found:

> *I have not attempted to go back any farther than Andreus &mdash; my goal at the start was to find the first Wildermuth in America.... I don't know who his father was or his mother. Andreus, our fourth great-grandfather. I do know that he was the father of Adam Wildermuth, and that he was a gartner (vineyard gardener) in Rielingshausen.*

The FamilySearch family tree later answered Robert Earl's open question: Johann Adam (1720&ndash;1768) and Rosina Kreher (b. 1721), three generations further back into the Neckar valley.
