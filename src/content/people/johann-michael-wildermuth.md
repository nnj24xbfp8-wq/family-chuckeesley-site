Johann Michael Wildermuth was born around **1831** in **[Grossaspach](/places/grossaspach/)** &mdash; the small Württemberg village northeast of Stuttgart whose parish records, eventually consulted by a Grossaspach genealogist at the request of the city's mayor in the 1980s, confirmed his birth there.

## The 1847 emigration

He emigrated to America in **1847** at age **sixteen**, traveling with the **Rossar family** &mdash; **William Rossar of the Duchy of Baden** and his wife **Frederika of Württemberg** (possibly Johann Michael's sister, per Robert Earl's reading). They arrived in **the Port of New York City** &mdash; not Philadelphia, as Robert Earl had initially assumed.

The detail is itself a piece of Robert Earl's research story. He had spent years writing to the Philadelphia city archivist looking for the naturalization paperwork. In **August 1986**, three years after his last letter to Philadelphia, he received an unexpected reply: the record *had* been found &mdash; in **New York**. *"How the archivist remembered the name after three years is a mystery to me,"* Robert Earl wrote, *"but I was most grateful to receive it."*

## Marietta, Ohio &mdash; shoemaker

By **the 1860 U.S. Census** he had reached the **3rd Ward of Marietta City, Washington County, Ohio**, the Ohio River town at the confluence of the Ohio and Muskingum rivers &mdash; the same Marietta that would be the family's geographic center for the next 150 years. The census recorded him as:

> *John M. Wildermuth, age 29, a shoemaker from Wuerrttemburg, Germany*

Living in the household of **William Rossar** (38, shoemaker from Baden) and his wife **Frederika** (from Württemberg). The Rossars were running a shoemaking business; Johann Michael was working for or with them. Whether the Württemberg Rossar-Wildermuth tie was sibling or in-law is the open question.

## Naturalization

He filed his **declaration of intention to become an American citizen on 1 November 1892** &mdash; recorded in the Philadelphia Naturalization Records, Book III. Whether he ever completed the citizenship process is not certain; Robert Earl's note: *"either he didn't take out citizenship in Philadelphia, or as many immigrants he simply did not complete all of the steps necessary."*

## His descendants

The Marietta marriage and birth records that Robert Earl found in the Washington County Court House established the basic outlines of his family forward; the specific names and dates of his wife and children are the queued research follow-up. He was [Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/)'s **great-grandfather** &mdash; making him **Chuck's great-great-great-grandfather** on the maternal Wildermuth line, through the chain:

> Johann Michael &rarr; (unconfirmed) &rarr; **John Wildermuth** (Robert Earl's grandfather) &rarr; **[Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/)** &rarr; **[Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/)** &rarr; **[Terrie Lee Wildermuth Eesley](/family/terrie-lee-eesley/)** &rarr; Chuck.

The deeper Wildermuth roots in Rielingshausen run back through **[Adam Wildermuth](/family/adam-wildermuth/)** (b. 1768) to **[Andreus Wildermuth](/family/andreus-wildermuth/)** (b. ~1748, the deepest documented ancestor).

## Queued

- **The New York 1847 passenger manifest** &mdash; Robert Earl flagged this as his last unfinished line of research. Sometimes the manifests gave a physical description; if found, this would be the only documented physical portrait of Johann Michael.
- **His marriage and children's records** from the Marietta Probate Court &mdash; Robert Earl consulted these but the specific names did not make it into the final write-up.
- **His relationship to Frederika Rossar** &mdash; sibling, in-law, or unrelated travel companion?
