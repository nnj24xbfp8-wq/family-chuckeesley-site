He was the **Wildermuth who left.**

He was born around **1831** in **[Grossaspach](/places/grossaspach/)** — one of the small Neckar-valley wine villages in Württemberg, ten kilometers from [Rielingshausen](/places/rielingshausen-church/) where his great-great-great-grandfather [Andreus the *Gartner*](/family/andreus-wildermuth/) had tended the vineyards eighty years earlier. The Wildermuths had been in this part of Württemberg, by the time he was born, for **at least six documented generations** running back to **[Johannes Wildermuth](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1650/)** in 1650.

He sailed in **1847**, age **sixteen**.

## What it would have meant to leave

To be sixteen in Württemberg in 1847 was to have grown up inside a particular set of pressures: a population that had recovered from the Napoleonic wars and was now larger than the available farmland could comfortably feed, an agricultural economy that depended on the dwindling availability of new vineyard land in already-tight valleys, an inheritance system (partible inheritance under Württemberg customary law) that split holdings smaller and smaller with every generation, and a German political world that was about to convulse in the **revolutions of 1848 / 49**. He left a year before those revolutions broke, and a year before the failed revolutions sent the much larger wave of *Forty-Eighter* German political refugees to America. He was an *economic* emigrant, not a political one — a sixteen-year-old leaving a vineyard village for somewhere there was room to make a different kind of life.

The journey he made — Grossaspach across to the Atlantic coast (probably **Bremen** or **Hamburg**, the two main embarkation ports for German emigrants of that era), six to eight weeks across the Atlantic in steerage, arrival in **New York** — would have been the great event of his life. He arrived with a family from a neighboring German region, the **Rossars of Baden** — **William Rossar**, a shoemaker, and his wife **Frederika** of Württemberg. Robert Earl Wildermuth's 1990 research suggested Frederika may have been Johann Michael's sister, in which case the Rossar journey was an extended-family undertaking: a married older sister, her husband, and the teenage younger brother. They came inland together — by canal-and-river to the Ohio valley, almost certainly, given the era and the routes — and ended up in **Marietta, Ohio**, the small Ohio River town at the confluence of the Ohio and Muskingum.

## The Marietta shoemaker

By the **1860 census**, he was twenty-nine, recorded as **John M. Wildermuth, age 29, a shoemaker from Wuerrttemburg, Germany**, living in the household of William Rossar (38, shoemaker, Baden) and Frederika Rossar (Württemberg). The two men were working together at the shoe trade. Whether Johann Michael had learned shoemaking in Grossaspach or in Marietta is not documented; the Rossar-Wildermuth household was running it together.

To be a small-town shoemaker in mid-19th-century Marietta was a trade with steady demand. Pre-factory shoes were custom-made, the leather worked over wooden lasts to fit each customer's feet, the soles hand-stitched in waxed thread. A skilled shoemaker could turn out a pair of boots every two or three days. His customers were river boatmen, the dock workers, the farmers from the surrounding hill country who came into town on market days, the merchants and lawyers and ministers of the Ohio River town. He spoke the German of his home and the English he had picked up in his teens.

His **declaration of intention to become an American citizen** was filed on **1 November 1892** — at age sixty-one, forty-five years after he had arrived. Robert Earl's note on the citizenship paperwork: "either he didn't take out citizenship in Philadelphia [Robert Earl's initial assumption] or as many immigrants he simply did not complete all of the steps necessary." A long-deferred administrative loose end.

He married, raised children in Marietta, and lived out the rest of his life in the river town. The specific names of his wife and children — the intermediate generations between him and Robert Earl Wildermuth's own grandfather John — are the open work in this archive's Wildermuth genealogy, queued for research against the Washington County, Ohio probate court records that Robert Earl consulted in the 1970s.

## The arc the photograph the family doesn't have

Robert Earl's closing note in 1990:

> *Someday, time permitting, perhaps I can check through the ship arrivals in New York City to find the ship and its port of debarkation in Germany. Sometimes ship's passenger lists give a physical description of the passenger. That would be interesting for I have no picture of Johann.*

The archive does not yet have a photograph of Johann Michael. The 1847 manifest, if found, would be the closest thing.
