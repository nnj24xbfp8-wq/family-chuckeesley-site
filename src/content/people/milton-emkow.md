**Ball turret gunner** of Robert Earl Wildermuth's B-24, 400th Squadron, 90th Bomb Group, Pacific theater 1944–45. From **Madison, Wisconsin**.

The **ball turret** was the Plexiglas sphere on the underside of the B-24, where the gunner sat **curled fetal-position** with twin .50-caliber machine guns covering the lower hemisphere of the aircraft. It was the most **physically constricted and exposed** combat position on a B-24, requiring smaller-bodied men. The position has its own American literary memorial in **Randall Jarrell's 1945 poem "The Death of the Ball Turret Gunner"** — six lines that became the most-anthologized American war poem of the 20th century:

> *From my mother's sleep I fell into the State,*
> *And I hunched in its belly till my wet fur froze.*
> *Six miles from earth, loosed from its dream of life,*
> *I woke to black flak and the nightmare fighters.*
> *When I died they washed me out of the turret with a hose.*

Milton Emkow's position on Robert Earl's crew was the position Jarrell wrote that poem about. He **survived the tour**; per Robert Earl's [memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/), all ten of the crew came home.

He is named in Robert Earl's [1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/) and the [combat log *The Big One*](/docs/the-big-one/). His pre-war Wisconsin life and post-war return are open research.

> *Sources: [Robert Earl Wildermuth's 1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/); [The Big One](/docs/the-big-one/).*
