He was born in **1650** in the Württemberg countryside, almost certainly in or near **[Rielingshausen](/places/rielingshausen-church/)** in the Neckar river valley northeast of Stuttgart — the village whose vineyards his great-grandson [Andreus](/family/andreus-wildermuth/) would tend a century later. The Württemberg he was born into was **two years past the end of the Thirty Years' War**.

That war is most of the context for understanding Johannes's life. From 1618 to 1648, central Europe had been the theatre of a religious and dynastic conflict that killed somewhere between **a quarter and a third of the German-speaking population** — historians' estimates vary, but the destruction was unmatched in European history until the world wars of the 20th century. **Württemberg was one of the worst-hit regions**, lying on the routes the imperial, Swedish, French, and Bavarian armies marched along between major theatres. Villages were burned. Vineyards were salted or simply abandoned for years. Plague followed the armies. Some Württemberg villages lost three-quarters of their population.

Johannes was born into the **rebuilding generation**. His parents and grandparents had lived through the war; he and his peers spent their lives putting their corner of the Neckar valley back together. The vineyards along the Neckar that his descendants would tend across the next four generations had to be **replanted from rootstock**, often by the same families who had abandoned them. The village rolls had to be reconstructed from memory because so many parish registers had burned. The Lutheran reformed church of Württemberg — the religious institution his line would belong to from this point forward — was still consolidating its position.

He married **[Anna Metzger](/family/anna-metzger/)**, born the same year. The marriage produced at least one son, **[Johannes Wildermuth Jr.](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1682/)**, born 1682. There were likely more children whose names this archive does not yet hold; large families were the norm and the period's mortality made the production of surviving heirs an iterated process.

He died in **1694**, age **44** — young by 20th-century standards but unremarkable in the 17th-century countryside, where adult life expectancy after surviving childhood was roughly the late fifties for those spared illness, war, and accident. Anna outlived him by **thirty-four years**. The Württemberg widow's life would have included continuing to work the family plot, raising their surviving children, possibly remarrying, and aging into a position of household authority that 17th-century rural society generally extended to widows of standing. She was sixty-six when their son's son [Johann Adam](/family/johann-adam-wildermuth-1720/) was born in 1720; whether she lived to see her great-grandson [Andreus](/family/andreus-wildermuth/) (born about 1748) is doubtful, but possible.
