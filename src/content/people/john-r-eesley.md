He was the **eldest**.

He was born in Canada — at **Ayr** or **Hamilton**, in the early years of his parents' marriage, before his father [Albert Robert Sr](/family/albert-robert-eesley/) had started traveling Michigan to build and run mills. The family was settled in Ontario then, the father working for the Goldie family's flour mill, the mother [Jennie Goldie](/family/jeanie-goldie/) in her parents' world, the two of them new parents in their late twenties. Two children came in those Ontario years: John R. first, then his sister Anna (the future [Mrs. Scoon](/family/anna-eesley-scoon/)). After those two, Albert Robert started moving — Michigan towns, mill jobs, eventually Detroit and the eight further children, eventually Toronto and a permanent return to Canada in retirement.

## The miller

John R. took up the family trade. Per [William Eesley's c. 1916 obituary](/docs/william-eesley-obituary-college-corner/), he was the first of the **six Eesley brothers who became millers** — *"John R., who follows milling"* is how the obituary names him, set apart from his nine siblings in the listing because he was the eldest. The trade he learned was the trade his father had learned and his grandfather had learned and his great-great-grandfather John Eesley of Hanwell, Oxfordshire had learned: the management of grain through the conversion to flour, with the millstones turning under wind or water or (by John's time) steam power, the bran and the meal sorted, the local farmers' grain weighed in and the flour weighed out.

To be a journeyman miller in late-Victorian Ontario or Michigan was a respectable trade in a small mill town. You worked long days during harvest. You knew the local farmers. You understood the seasonal rhythm of grain coming in and flour going out. Where your specific mill stood — a Canadian crossroads town, a Michigan grain-belt junction — would have shaped what your daily life looked like, but the work was the same.

## In the family portrait

He stands in the back row of the **[1899 family group portrait](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-1899/)** — labeled simply "John (oldest)" in the family caption — a man in his late thirties or early forties by then, the senior son in the studio frame with his siblings and his parents. The "oldest" parenthetical is its own quiet honor; in a family of ten children, eldest sibling is a status the family marks.

His marriage, children (if any), and the place where his milling career ended are the queued research follow-ups against the 1900 and 1910 U.S. and Canadian censuses.
