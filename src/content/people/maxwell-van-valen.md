He was **Robert Earl Wildermuth's pilot** for the entire combat tour with the **400th Squadron, 90th Bomb Group ("Jolly Rogers")** in the Pacific, 1944–45. From **Newton, Massachusetts** and **MIT-educated** — the most formally educated man on the crew at a time when an MIT engineering degree was rare in the Army Air Corps. Per Robert Earl's [memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/), Van Valen used to **turn the controls of the B-24 over to the co-pilot and crawl down into the nose of the aircraft** where Robert Earl was navigating, to watch the navigation calculations and marvel when they ended up where they were supposed to be — *"flying over long distances of ocean where there were no radio stations or landmarks made a believer in the techniques of navigation out of him."*

The night Robert Earl got lost over the South China Sea — the lightning thunderhead Lt. Adler had mistaken for the home airstrip radar return, the Japanese decoy radio bearings, the U.S. naval convoy that gave them a heading home — Van Valen was the pilot wrestling the controls back to Tacloban on the last fifteen minutes of fuel. *"As we were taxiing in to our parking place on the airstrip, the two outboard engines stopped running — out of fuel."*

He is named in Robert Earl's [combat log *The Big One*](/docs/the-big-one/) crew roster. His post-war life, his marriage, his children, and any reunion attempts with Robert Earl after the war are open research.

> *Sources: [Robert Earl Wildermuth's 1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/); [The Big One](/docs/the-big-one/).*
