## Coraopolis, Bethel, Penn State

Peggy was born **September 10, 1914 in Coraopolis, Pennsylvania** &mdash; the small borough on the Ohio River just northwest of Pittsburgh &mdash; to [Robert T. McMaster](/family/) (civil engineer) and [Alice Anderson McMaster](/family/) (schoolteacher). She graduated from **Bethel High School (Bethel, Pennsylvania)** in **1930** &mdash; finishing high school not quite sixteen &mdash; and from **Pennsylvania State University** in **1936**, having gone up at seventeen and joined **Phi Mu Sorority** there. The line about the Penn State years that survived into the family memory: *"I never lacked for a dance partner."* It is the only surviving sentence from before Will, and it sits in the record the way her dry humor later would &mdash; one phrase that says exactly the kind of person she had been.

## The Home Ec classroom

She taught **Home Economics in Butler, Pennsylvania** in the years after Penn State, and again at **Marietta High School in the early 1960s** &mdash; her teaching career bracketed her years raising the four children, the way a generation of educated American women's careers were often bracketed in mid-century. Home Economics was, in the secondary-school curriculum of the time, the practical-craft discipline that taught budgeting, cooking, household running, and the daily-life skills that schools then thought worth teaching to the generation about to set up its own households. (The continuity with the **investing** she is best remembered for in this archive is exact: a household economist on the home side, a portfolio strategist on the capital side &mdash; one practice, two scales.)

## The 7th Street house, marriage, the children

Peggy McMaster Eesley married Will on June 20, 1942 and raised four children with him in Columbus and then Marietta, settling into the **7th Street house** that would be Grandma Peggy's house in the next generation's memory.

She is the figure who turns out to have done the most to shape the next two generations of Eesley life, not from any public role but through the sustained, patient practice of long-term investing. Chuck's eulogy for his father puts it most simply:

> *"It was her wisdom, far-sightedness and patience in investing that inspired him to become a stockbroker, funded my grandparents' travel in retirement, and paid for a large part of my education. Phone calls with dad always included an update of how the stock market was doing recently and a request that I use my education to make it go up again soon."*

Her dark, dry sense of humor went directly to her son Charlie, who passed a more diluted version to Chuck. *"I failed to inherit the full dose of the wicked sense of humor my dad got from my grandmother,"* Chuck said in the eulogy. *"That's why I had to become a professor where the bar for being funny is sufficiently low."*

## The grandmother Chuck knew

The 7th Street house is also the house **Chuck spent every weekday afternoon in through his childhood.** He went there after school and stayed until his father got off work and the two of them drove home; one or two nights a week the three of them &mdash; Peggy, Charlie, Chuck &mdash; would stay for dinner there too. The "Grandma Peggy" of the next generation's memory is the figure who set that table, week after week, year after year, across Chuck's elementary through high-school years.

She **played bridge** in her later years, regularly and seriously &mdash; the after-Will, into-her-90s decades, when the McMaster patience-and-strategy side of her was running on cards instead of capital but on the same operating system.

Outside the bridge table she was an active member of the **PEO** (Philanthropic Educational Organization &mdash; UM chapter), **The Betsey Mills Club**, **First Congregational United Church of Christ** (where she served as director of Christian education and was a member of the Dana Philathia Class), and the **Junior Reading Club** that she and Will had both been part of. The obituary's own closing characterization &mdash; *"remembered for her optimistic outlook and ready smile"* &mdash; is the line that runs underneath all of it.

## The end

In her final days her son Charlie would come from work to her house on 7th Street and feed her dinner himself, every day. The eulogy treats this as the quiet evidence of who they had been to each other. The same table Chuck had been doing homework at after school for fifteen years &mdash; the same table the two of them sat at for dinner once or twice a week &mdash; was where Charlie sat with her at the end.

She died **May 21, 2007 at 11:30 p.m. at Harmar Place**, the Marietta facility she had moved to from Seventh Street toward the end. Age **92**. She was preceded in death by her husband Wilbur "Will" Chenoweth Eesley (d. June 2, 1986; they had been married forty-four years) and by her brother **Don McMaster**. She was survived by her four children &mdash; [Jeanne Eesley Kamiab](/family/jeanne-eesley-kamiab/) and Joe of Cleveland, [Anne Eesley](/family/anne-eesley/) of Spring Lakes, Michigan, [Charles Eesley](/family/charles-eesley/) and Diana of Lowell, and Margaret "Maggie" Eesley of Forrest, Virginia &mdash; her four grandchildren (Stephanie Kamiab Mullin, **Chuck Eesley**, Amina Rubin, and Nooreen Rubin), and a great-grandson, Cameron Mullin.

A memorial service was held at **First Congregational Church** in Marietta on the Friday after her death, with the Rev. Linda Steelman officiating &mdash; the same church where she had taught Christian education for years, and where Charlie would later be eulogized by his own son in late 2015.

She died before Chuck finished the MIT PhD in 2009 and before he took the Stanford appointment in the years after. The arc of the four-generation chain &mdash; Will and Peggy buying her investments in Marietta, those returns paying for Chuck's education, Chuck eventually onto the Stanford faculty &mdash; she completed her half of by the time she went.

The McMaster and Anderson lines on Peggy's side &mdash; Robert Thompson McMaster the civil engineer, Alice Anderson McMaster the schoolteacher, Gilbert Clement and Margaret Thompson McMaster, Rev. Abraham Ramsey Anderson and Nancy Jane Shaw, plus Donald A. McMaster who ran the northeastern U.S. communications power grid in WWII &mdash; are documented in Bean's 1985 register and queued as future entries.
