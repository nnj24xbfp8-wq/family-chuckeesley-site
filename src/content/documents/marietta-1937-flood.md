## The flood

The **January–February 1937 Ohio River flood** was the **highest crest in the river's recorded history** — sixty-three feet at Cincinnati on 26 January 1937, well above the previous 1884 record. The river overflowed for nearly a thousand miles from Pittsburgh to Cairo, Illinois; nearly a million people were displaced; over 350 deaths; property damage in the hundreds of millions of 1937 dollars. **Marietta, Ohio sits at the confluence of the Muskingum and Ohio rivers** and was directly in the flood's path — the city's lower-lying neighborhoods went under, and the 1937 high-water marks are still visible on Marietta buildings today.

The Wildermuth family's house at **[413 Harmar Street](/places/413-harmar-street-marietta/)** — three blocks back from the Muskingum, on a brick foundation four feet high — flooded to the first-floor ceiling line. Robert Earl was twelve, going on thirteen.

## In Robert Earl's own words

The flood is the most vivid disaster scene in Robert Earl's [1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/):

> *"As the rivers slowly rose, all people living in houses that were threatened with flooding, moved their furniture to the second floor of their houses and then found friends or relatives on higher ground with whom they could live while the high waters were up. This was usually about two weeks. Of course flood time was a big occasion for us young boys and we paddled around in our father's boats and 'rescued' stranded people or took them to inspect their flooded homes. All for a fee of course."*

The signature image of the family's flood — the wooden fireplace mantle floating off its base, banging around inside the house breaking every window — is the kind of specific physical detail that survives across the half-century into the memoir:

> *"The water was just deep enough to float a wooden mantle over our fireplace off its foundation and the large mantle was floating around in the house breaking all of the windows."*

## The paperboy's flood

The flood was also Robert Earl's first **commercial enterprise**. He had a paper route already; during the flood, he reconfigured it as a boat route:

> *"I had a paper route so I would take our family's boat over to the Marietta City district and get my papers from the local newspaper office that remained in operation on their second floor and my paper selling business was extremely large. I could sell more papers than the news company could provide me. Everybody wanted a paper to see what the predictions were for the rising river waters to start receding."*

The image — twelve-year-old Robert Earl rowing his father's boat across the Muskingum into a flooded downtown Marietta to pick up the day's newspapers from a second-floor newsroom, then rowing back through the West Side and selling them at floodwater-premium prices door-to-door — is the founding scene of his later business sense. The same instinct (find a service nobody else can deliver; do it; charge fairly) would show up forty years later in his post-Air Force second career as a Blue Cross of Florida medical-records auditor.

## What survived in the family memory

Beyond his own family's flood, Robert Earl notes the broader Marietta pattern:

> *"Actually the flood waters were only in our house once while we lived there but almost every year, the flood waters would get up to our street. The old buildings in the Marietta business district, most of them built in the 1800's, have survived many, many floods with water up as high as ten to fifteen feet inside them and at this date many of them are still in use."*

The annual rise of the rivers — flood-water-on-our-street as routine — is the Marietta-river-town life detail his Wildermuth-side childhood was built on. The 1937 flood was the **one event when the water came inside the house**; the rest were near-misses Robert Earl took for granted.
