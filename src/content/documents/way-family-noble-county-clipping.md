The **"Way family among early settlers in Noble County"** newspaper clipping &mdash; written by Burns Harlan, *Times* Regional Editor &mdash; is the central biographical record of the [English-immigrant Way family](/family/edward-taylor-way/) that brings the Way name into Chuck's maternal Davis line. The clipping has been preserved in Chuck's family papers with **handwritten margin annotations** by an earlier-generation family genealogist, settling several disputed family details. It is one of the four key family-papers documents on the Davis-Way side of the archive (alongside the [Anderson 1894 letter](/docs/anderson-1894-letter-to-margaret/), the [Wildermuth-Fleming Heritage](/docs/wildermuth-fleming-heritage-1990/), and the [Belle Way 1901 wedding portrait](/archive/belle-way-howard-wickens-wedding-1901/)).

## What the clipping documents

The clipping draws on the 1887 *Watkins's History of Noble County* in describing the Way family as part of the **early English-immigrant farming generation** that broke ground in southeastern Ohio in the 1840s and 1850s. Its substantive content covers:

- **[Edward Taylor Way](/family/edward-taylor-way/)** (1812-1879) &mdash; born on the **Isle of Wight, England**, the founding emigrant of the Way family branch in Noble County, Ohio. The Isle of Wight is a thirty-mile-wide chalk-down island off the south coast of Hampshire, four miles across the Solent from Portsmouth &mdash; one of the great working-class agricultural and naval emigrant counties of mid-nineteenth-century England.
- **[Anna Ellison](/family/anna-ellison/)** (b. 1821, Coppull, Lancashire, England) &mdash; Edward's wife, of the Lancashire cotton-mill belt south of Preston. The Way-Ellison marriage of **27 January 1845 at Morgan County, Ohio** is the Isle-of-Wight-meets-Lancashire crossing that founded the Way line in Ohio.
- **The nine Way-Ellison children**: *Elizabeth, David R., Mary A., Abagail, Edward E., Henry, Sarah E., Emily J. and William M.*

## The handwritten margin annotation

A handwritten note on the clipping, in an earlier-generation family hand, annotates the eldest daughter:

> *"Elizabeth, the oldest, was married twice. Her first husband was Lindley Yarnall."*

The Yarnall name brings a Quaker-and-Welsh-origin Pennsylvania-and-Ohio surname into the Way-Ellison story &mdash; a research thread for the eldest Way daughter's marriages.

## What it does NOT document — the next generation forward to Chuck

The clipping covers **Edward Taylor Way's generation and his children**. It does NOT directly enumerate the next generation (the grandchildren), through which Chuck's direct line runs:

- **[Edward E. Way](/family/edward-e-way/)** (1851-1924) &mdash; Edward Taylor's eldest known son, married [Tacy Elizabeth Matthews](/family/tacy-elizabeth-matthews/) in 1872 and fathered ten children
- **[Victoria Anna Way Davis](/family/victoria-anna-way-davis/)** (1874-1903) &mdash; Edward E.'s eldest daughter, **Chuck's maternal great-great-grandmother**, who married [William Armstrong Davis](/family/william-armstrong-davis/) and became the mother of [Homer Edward Davis](/family/homer-davis/)
- **[Belsora "Belle" Way Wickens](/family/belsora-way-wickens/)** (1880-1912) &mdash; Edward E.'s younger daughter, whose [25 September 1901 wedding portrait](/archive/belle-way-howard-wickens-wedding-1901/) is the family-papers document that surfaces the rest of this generation

## A clarification June 2026

Earlier passes of this archive misread the clipping &mdash; in the absence of the family-papers wedding portrait, I had inferred that Victoria Anna Way (born 1874) was the *daughter* of Edward Taylor Way directly. The [1901 wedding portrait identification legend](/archive/belle-way-howard-wickens-wedding-1901/), most likely annotated by Dorothy Marie Davis Wildermuth (Chuck's maternal grandmother), labels **Edward E. Way as "great-grandfather"** and **Tacy Matthews as "great-grandmother"** &mdash; making them Victoria's parents and Edward Taylor + Anna Ellison her grandparents. The GEDCOM trace (June 2026) confirms this generational placement.

The clipping is therefore **the biographical document of Chuck's maternal three-times-great-grandparents and four-times-great-grandparents** &mdash; the deep-time English emigration of the Way line into Ohio. It is the documentary anchor of the Way-side family of [Crooked Tree Cemetery, Noble County, Ohio](/family/edward-e-way/), where four generations of the line are buried.
