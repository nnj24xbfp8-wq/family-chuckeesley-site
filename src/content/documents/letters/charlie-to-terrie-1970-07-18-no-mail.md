## What the letter is

18 July 1970. Charlie writes Terrie a short, angry letter from somewhere in the I Corps field, mid-tour. He has just been resupplied by helicopter for another seven days in the bush; mail caught up with the resupply and he had letters from five different people but nothing from her; the captain has decided the unit is not coming in. The letter is short, blunt, and has a register Charlie does not often let himself write in. It is one of the small honest moments in the corpus.

## Transcription

> *18 July*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> Just a short sad note. I got mail from 5 different people yesterday but none from you. I trust nothing is wrong. I have received more mail from you than anyone else with my parents a close second. I really want your letters although I realize there may be all sort of complications.
>
> It seems that our gung-ho captain won't let us go in. We get resupplied for 7 more days of this drudgery out here. From the look of things I guess you will have to wait about [a week or two for] next mail. From what I can gather I have several packages in there I can't get to. I am angry.
>
> By the way, you may as well send your letters with 6 cent stamps than airmail and save yourself 4 cents. Screw the gov't anyway you can.

## What the letter records

**The five letters, none from Terrie.** *"I got mail from 5 different people yesterday but none from you."* The opening line is one of the more emotionally exposed in the corpus. Charlie's whole structural relationship with mail had been that Terrie was the most-frequent sender, with his parents [Will](/family/wilbur-eesley/) and [Peggy](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) a close second. A helicopter resupply that brought letters from five other people but nothing from Terrie was a small piece of personal-mail bad news. *"I trust nothing is wrong"* &mdash; he hedges, but the line *"I really want your letters"* is the more honest one. Charlie is in the bush, and Terrie's letters are the structural reason he can keep going. The 18 July 1970 break in her mail flow was felt.

**The gung-ho captain.** *"It seems that our gung-ho captain won't let us go in. We get resupplied for 7 more days of this drudgery out here."* The captain-level dynamic that runs through the field rotations of Charlie's corpus &mdash; some captains rotated their units back to the firebase on the normal cycle; others were the *"gung-ho"* type who pushed for extended bush time to build the unit's combat record. Charlie's anger here is the small unfiltered note: he was a draftee, he didn't want extra time in the field, and the captain's career-incentive decision was being absorbed by the soldiers under him. *"From the look of things I guess you will have to wait about a week or two for next mail."* The seven more days of drudgery would mean another seven days of no letters back home, which compounded the personal-mail problem with the gung-ho problem.

**'Screw the gov't anyway you can.'** *"By the way, you may as well send your letters with 6 cent stamps than airmail and save yourself 4 cents. Screw the gov't anyway you can."* The line is one of the most populist register-notes in the corpus. Domestic First Class postage in July 1970 was **6 cents** per ounce (after the [March 1968 rate increase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_United_States_postage_rates)); airmail to APO addresses overseas ran 10 cents. The 4-cent savings was trivial economically, but Charlie's read of the gesture is what makes the line: he was a drafted soldier writing to his girlfriend from a Vietnam field rotation suggesting she stiff the federal mail service for four cents on every letter to him, because the same federal government compelling his presence in the field was also profiting from the postage premium for getting her letters to him. *"Screw the gov't anyway you can"* is the philosophy in compressed form. It is the closest the corpus comes to the broader anti-war / anti-government register of late-1960s and early-1970s draftee culture in Charlie's own voice, applied to the mundane question of stamp rates.

**The packages he can't reach.** *"From what I can gather I have several packages in there I can't get to."* Mail to a deployed soldier ran through the rear-area APO; packages from family stacked up at the rear waiting for the soldier to rotate back. Charlie knew, from secondary chatter on the helicopter resupply line, that he had multiple packages waiting for him &mdash; possibly from his mother Peggy's care-package routine, possibly from Terrie, possibly from [Dorothy Hauser](/family/dorothy-hauser/) and the Mothers for Peace women &mdash; and he couldn't reach them because the captain wouldn't let the unit come in. The packages, like the mail-flow break with Terrie, were one more layer of the underlying frustration of the 18 July day.
