## What the letter is

10 February 1971. Charlie writes a short steady letter from Da Nang to Terrie at Maryland. The same day his mother [Peggy McMaster Eesley](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) writes [her own 10 February 1971 letter](/docs/letters/peggy-to-terrie-1971-02-10/) from Marietta to Terrie at Maryland, enclosing the final letter from the CO at Camp Eagle to Congressman Clarence Miller about Charlie's tour situation.

The two letters together — Charlie's and Peggy's, from opposite sides of the world on the same day, both addressed to Terrie — are the archive's most concentrated single-day record of the family's parallel work on Charlie's tour endgame.

## Transcription

> *Feb 10*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> Well not much happening now. I'm still in Danang and I hope my orders come down so I can transfer to my clerks job. I'm better off down here but up there I'll at least have the guarantee of not seeing the field.
>
> People are starting to get on my nerves. I guess I'm just getting jittery about coming home.
>
> After this big push in Laos I believe we will then start pulling out in a big way. I'll not get out til Jan 14 but at least I'll be free.
>
> I guess this letter sounds rather repetitious. I just can't think of much to say.
>
> Love Charles

## What the letter records

**The clerk's job.** Charlie is waiting for orders to transfer to a clerk's billet in the rear so that whatever remains of his Vietnam time will be guaranteed off the field — a desk job at Camp Eagle or similar. The transfer doesn't carry the same risk reduction as the rear-area Da Nang assignment he is on right now, but it is the structural arrangement that takes him off field operations for good.

**Lam Son 719 — "this big push in Laos."** *"After this big push in Laos I believe we will then start pulling out in a big way."* The big push Charlie names is **Operation Lam Son 719**, the South Vietnamese ARVN ground invasion of southern Laos that began 8 February 1971 — two days before this letter — with U.S. air support but no U.S. ground troops permitted west of the Vietnamese border. The 101st Airborne, Charlie's division, provided some of the helicopter lift and ground-fire support from the I Corps side of the border. Lam Son 719 was the test case for Vietnamization — whether the ARVN could conduct a major offensive on its own with American air and logistics backing. The operation became the bloody hinge of the late war; Charlie's prediction that the U.S. would pull out "in a big way" after it was the conventional wisdom in the field at the time.

**The "Jan 14" date.** *"I'll not get out til Jan 14."* Charlie's [19 January 1971 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-01-19-ets-extension/) had laid out the strategy: extend Vietnam tour by ~32 days to ETS from Vietnam rather than serve stateside. The new exit date of January 14 in this letter is presumably his projected discharge under that extension strategy. ([The ETS-from-Vietnam plan ultimately collapsed against the U.S. drawdown timetable; Charlie returned to the States in 1971 — exact date open research.)

**"People are starting to get on my nerves. I guess I'm just getting jittery about coming home."** The shortest characterization of late-tour mood. The combat phase is essentially over for him; what is left is the rear-area waiting for orders, and the ordinary human nerves at the end of a long deployment.

## The pairing with Peggy's letter

What makes this letter structurally important in the archive is the **single-day co-arrival** with Peggy's congressional letter. Both letters were written 10 February 1971; both went to Terrie at Maryland; both addressed the same tour endgame. **Charlie was working it from the field; Peggy was working it through Congress.** The archive's two opposite-end primary sources line up on one day.
