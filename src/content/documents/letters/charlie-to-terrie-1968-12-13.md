## What the letter is

**13 December 1968.** Charlie Eesley, twenty-one years old, in his senior year at [Marietta College](/family/charles-eesley/) on an economics track, sits down to write [Terrie Lee Wildermuth](/family/terrie-lee-eesley/) &mdash; the woman who will become his wife and Chuck's mother &mdash; about a Mark Twain book the bookstore didn't have, about kitchen duty, about Marshal Moist getting engaged to his pinmate, about last night's formal being a flop, and about the news he's most glad to hear: **she's been accepted into the University of Maryland.**

It is the **chronological start of the surviving Charlie-and-Terrie correspondence** in this archive. A year and some months later he will ship to Vietnam and start writing the [Letters from Vietnam](/docs/letters-from-vietnam/) sequence from Saigon. The voice that will write those is already on the page here: dry, observational, deferential to her news, attentive to the small absurdities (the engagement gossip, the formal that flopped), one step less serious about himself than he is about her.

## Transcription

> *12/13/68*
>
> Dear Terry,
>
> They will be late for your birthday and so will your present. I was going to give you Twain's book *"Letters from the Earth"* but the book store didn't have it. You might get it later.
>
> What can I say that might interest you? The kitchen is dirty again. Marshal Moist got engaged to his pinmate. (how does that grab you?) He gets a lot of grief for it. We had a formal last night and it was sort of a flop.
>
> I'm glad to hear you were accepted into Maryland.
>
> [page break / further passages on subsequent pages of this letter not yet placed in archive]

## Things worth noting

- He addresses her as **"Terry,"** not "Terrie" &mdash; an early-correspondence informality that won't stick. By the Vietnam letters he writes "Terrie."
- The Twain book he meant to send &mdash; ***Letters from the Earth*** &mdash; is Twain's posthumously published collection of skeptical theological essays (Satan writes letters from earth to other angels in heaven about the strange habits of humans). It is the book Charlie thought twenty-one-year-old Terrie would find interesting on her birthday. The choice is its own small portrait of who he thought she was.
- **Terrie was accepted into the University of Maryland** in December 1968 &mdash; presumably for the spring or fall 1969 semester. Her undergraduate years are documented elsewhere in this archive only sparsely; this is the earliest dated record of her academic plans surviving in the correspondence.
- The kitchen, the formal, Marshal Moist and his pinmate &mdash; this is fraternity life or dormitory life, the small social weather of an undergraduate Christmas-vacation lull. A year later he will be writing about something very different.
