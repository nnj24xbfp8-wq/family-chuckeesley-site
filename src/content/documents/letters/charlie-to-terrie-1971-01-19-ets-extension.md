## What the letter is

19 January 1971. Charlie writes Terrie from a rear-area Vietnam base — almost certainly Camp Eagle or Da Nang, given that he was pulled back from the field by this point in the tour. He has been waiting for word on what the Army's policy will be for early discharge ("drops") and has decided that if nothing definite comes through, he will take matters into his own hands.

The decision he is announcing is counterintuitive on first read: he is going to extend his Vietnam tour by **32 or 33 days** so he can ETS (Expiration Term of Service) directly from Vietnam rather than rotate back to the States and serve out the remainder of his Army time in garrison. The math is simple: a few extra weeks in Vietnam saves him months of stateside harassment. He has already heard from buddies that stateside duty is no better than another field tour.

## Transcription

> *Jan 19*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> I got 2 letters from you today. I still haven't heard any word on drops so I've made up my mind that if they don't tell me anything definite I'm going to extend 32 or 33 days so I ETS from here. I don't think I'm going to like stateside duty as from what I hear it is a lot of harassment.
>
> I figured that if I extend now I'll have 147 days left now and 129 when I finally get back to the field if they need us back as I hear they are. With leave and R&R I can kill about 30 days out of the field and a few other things should mean that at most I'll have about 80 possible days in the field and probably less. With the weather changing and little or no contact it really shouldn't be that bad. It all depends though on whether they say something definite about drops.
>
> I can drink over here anytime. There is a bar on base here and it is the only one we go to. Also the P.X. sells...

## What the letter records

**The ETS-from-Vietnam strategy.** The Army's standard practice was to rotate soldiers back from Vietnam at the end of their tour and then assign them to a stateside unit for the remainder of their Army time. For a draftee or short-tour enlistee, this meant several months of garrison duty after the tour — inspections, parades, training, the bureaucratic harassment Charlie names. Soldiers who could extend their Vietnam tour by enough days to cover their remaining Army time could skip the stateside step entirely and discharge directly from Vietnam. Charlie's 32-or-33-day extension is exactly that calculation.

**The 80-days-in-the-field number.** Charlie's math projects 147 days left in the army at the moment of writing, 129 once back from the field, and at most 80 days remaining in the field after leave, R&R, and other deductions. The framing is the same accountancy of the tour that runs through the corpus: every form of leave subtracted, every contact day counted, the goal to minimize exposure across the last stretch.

**The pairing with Peggy's letter.** Three weeks after this letter, Charlie's mother [Peggy McMaster Eesley](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) writes [her 10 February 1971 letter to Terrie](/docs/letters/peggy-to-terrie-1971-02-10/) enclosing a final letter from the CO at Camp Eagle to Congressman Clarence Miller. The Eesley family was working both the soldier's-side extension strategy (Charlie planning his own days) and the parental-side congressional intervention (Peggy writing to the family's U.S. Representative) in parallel. Charlie's January 19 calculation and Peggy's February 10 letter are the two ends of the same family strategy for the final months of the tour.

**The bar on base.** *"I can drink over here anytime. There is a bar on base here and it is the only one we go to."* Charlie is no longer in the field; he is in the rear, with a bar accessible. The rear-area location matches the later 4 February and 10 February 1971 letters that open *"I'm still in Danang."*
