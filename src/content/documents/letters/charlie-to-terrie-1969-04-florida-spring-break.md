## What the letter is

Early April 1969. Charlie is on Florida spring break of his **senior year at [Marietta College](/family/charles-eesley/)** &mdash; about eleven months before he would ship to Vietnam. The letter is short, sunburned, and full of the small piece of late-1960s American college life that survives in it.

The substantive content is in the second paragraph: Charlie has **a Federal Reserve System job interview in Cleveland that Friday, and a Sears interview in Philadelphia the following Monday and Tuesday**. He is on the standard senior-year-of-college job-application track that an economics-major Marietta College graduate would have been on in spring 1969 &mdash; and that the draft would intercept later in the year.

## Transcription

> Dear Terry,
>
> Here I am at 1:00 PM burnt to a crisp. This is an amazing place. Cops are everywhere. People get arrested right & left. I've seen quite a bit of drinking but some people don't quit. Some girl got run over on the beach yesterday. I don't know whether she made it or not.
>
> Say about the weekend of April 12-13. That Friday I have to go to Cleveland for an interview with the Federal Reserve System and then on Monday & Tuesday I have to be in Philadelphia for an interview with Sears. I don't know whether I'll be able to make it that weekend or not.
>
> I should have stayed home & had you come over. My forehead is peeling. See you later!
>
> Love,
> Charles

## What the letter records

**The Florida spring-break scene.** *"Cops are everywhere. People get arrested right & left. I've seen quite a bit of drinking but some people don't quit. Some girl got run over on the beach yesterday."* The Florida spring break of 1969 was the same era of American college tourism that produced the Fort Lauderdale beach scenes of the [*Where the Boys Are*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Where_the_Boys_Are_(1960_film)) cultural moment a decade earlier, now compounded by the late-1960s college-age drinking culture, increased local-police presence, and intermittent serious incidents on the sand. Charlie's read &mdash; *"some people don't quit"* &mdash; is the small ironic distance of an observer who is on the beach but not at the center of the spring-break excess. The line about the girl getting run over is the small piece of unsettling news Charlie filed away.

**The Federal Reserve interview.** *"That Friday I have to go to Cleveland for an interview with the Federal Reserve System."* The Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland was the regional Fed branch for Ohio, and the standard interview track for an Ohio economics-major undergraduate looking at a career in central banking or financial-system economics. Charlie's senior-year academic record was strong enough to land an interview at this level &mdash; the Fed did not interview every economics major. The interview itself was for an entry-level research-and-analysis position at the Cleveland Fed, the kind of job that would have set up a financial-economics career path through the 1970s.

**The Sears interview.** *"Then on Monday & Tuesday I have to be in Philadelphia for an interview with Sears."* Sears, Roebuck and Co. in 1969 was one of the largest American retailers, with a national executive-trainee program that recruited new college graduates for store-management and merchandising careers. The Philadelphia interview was at one of Sears's major regional offices. The contrast between the two interviews &mdash; central-bank economics analysis vs. national-retail management &mdash; tracks the breadth of paths open to a Marietta-College economics graduate in spring 1969.

**The career path that didn't happen.** Neither the Fed nor the Sears path ended up as Charlie's life. By March 1970 he was in country with the 101st Airborne in Vietnam; by June 1971 he was a discharged veteran with a Marietta College degree and the Army on his résumé; by the mid-1970s he had settled into the stockbroker work he would do for the rest of his career &mdash; with The Ohio Company and later Hazlett, Burt & Watson, both Ohio-region firms. The Cleveland Fed and Philadelphia Sears interviews of April 1969 sit on a different timeline of his life than the one that actually happened. **The April 1969 letter is the small piece of evidence that he was on a different career track before the draft intercepted it.**

**The opening salutation.** *"Dear Terry"* &mdash; the same 1968-1969 spelling of her name that the [October 1968 Vanilla Fudge letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1968-10-31-vanilla-fudge/) carries. The *Terrie* spelling that runs through the Vietnam letters was a later convention; the pre-army Charlie was writing to *Terry*.
