## What the letter is

31 December 1970. New Year's Eve. Charlie writes Terrie a short letter from somewhere in I Corps with the practical-and-tired register of a soldier who has just heard official-sounding news he's been waiting on for months: the **10-month tour-shortening drop is definite**. The Army had, by late 1970, formally shortened the standard 12-month Vietnam tour to 10 months as part of the Vietnamization-era troop drawdown; Charlie's deployment in March 1970 had put him in the cohort that would be eligible. By his 31 December calculation he would be home **sometime in March 1971**, three months before the eventual ETS he would target.

The letter is one of the small key moments of the corpus. It records the version of Charlie's post-tour future that he ultimately did not take. Three weeks later the [19 January 1971 ETS-extension strategy letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-01-19-ets-extension/) would record his decision to extend instead &mdash; trading the home-by-March possibility for the cleaner discharge-from-Vietnam path on 14 June 1971.

## Transcription

> *Dec 31*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> Well the days keep rolling by. I've heard the 10-month tour is definite so I should be home sometime in March. I'm really having a hard time writing letters down here. I owe at least 4 thank-you letters for packages sent to me and haven't even sent them. I can't really think of much to say except that I won't believe I'm coming home til I'm there.
>
> Don't worry, I'm still a good little boy. I trust you are being good too. A guy I met down here, and he is a nice guy &hellip;

## What the letter records

**The 10-month tour confirmation.** *"I've heard the 10-month tour is definite so I should be home sometime in March."* The 10-month tour was the Vietnamization-era policy that shortened Army deployment tours from the standard 12 months. By late 1970 the drawdown was political reality: combat units were being withdrawn on a published timetable that the Cambodia incursion and Kent State had accelerated, and the Army was clearing tour rosters of soldiers whose home-station replacements had been cancelled. The 10-month tour was both a morale gesture and a practical mechanism for reducing in-country head-count without retraining replacement cohorts.

For Charlie, the policy meant home in March 1971 instead of mid-1971. *"I should be home sometime in March"* &mdash; based on a March 1970 deployment, the 10-month drop puts him at mid-March 1971 give or take administrative slack. This was, on 31 December 1970, the most concrete piece of return-date information he had received in months.

**The decision he was about to walk away from.** Three weeks later, by the [19 January 1971 ETS-extension strategy letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-01-19-ets-extension/), Charlie had reasoned through the trade-off and chosen the other path. The 10-month drop would have put him home in mid-March 1971 with **about three months of garrison duty remaining stateside**. The ETS-extension strategy &mdash; staying in Vietnam an extra three months &mdash; would let him discharge directly from country on 14 June 1971 with no stateside garrison duty at all. The trade was three months in Vietnam now for three months *out* of the Army at the end.

This 31 December letter records the moment before that decision was made. Charlie had heard the 10-month drop was definite. He was, at this point, planning to take it.

**The 'I won't believe I'm coming home til I'm there' note.** *"I won't believe I'm coming home til I'm there."* The line is the small piece of soldier-realism that runs through the corpus. Charlie had been disappointed too many times by Army rumors and pending-decisions, the closest of which had been the late-1970 Hawaii-R&R cancellation rumor that the [Dec 16 1970 enlisted men's club letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-12-16-em-club-photo/) had registered. *"I've been disappointed too many times to really get my hopes too high but I'm still hoping"* &mdash; the same register, two weeks later in the same place.

**The 4 thank-you letters owed.** *"I owe at least 4 thank-you letters for packages sent to me and haven't even sent them."* The small piece of practical-life accounting. The corpus contains multiple Peggy-to-Charlie packages, several from Terrie, and at least one from [Dorothy Hauser](/family/dorothy-hauser/) per the mid-1970 letter that mentions her *"goodies in her letters."* Charlie's December backlog of unsent thank-you notes is the small inverse-corpus piece: the letters that should have existed and didn't.

Three weeks of more thinking, and the trade became clear to him: better to stay in Vietnam three more months and discharge cleanly than to come home and pull garrison duty. The 10-month drop was real; Charlie just chose not to take it.
