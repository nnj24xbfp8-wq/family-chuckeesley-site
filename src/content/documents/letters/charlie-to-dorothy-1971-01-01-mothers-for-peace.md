## What the letter is

1 January 1971. Charlie writes a short New Year's letter to his then-girlfriend Terrie's mother, [Dorothy Marie Davis Wildermuth](/family/dorothy-davis-wildermuth/), from guard duty at Da Nang. The letter is one of the only surviving pieces in this archive between Charlie and a Wildermuth in-law during the war. It is also the document that surfaces one of the most consequential political details in the family record of the period: Dorothy was a member of **Mothers for Peace**, an American anti-Vietnam-War mothers' organization, and Charlie addresses the group directly in his sign-off.

## Transcription

> *Jan 1*
>
> Dear Dorothy,
>
> Happy New Year! It is so hot over here and I'm on guard in Danang. I hope the generals don't feel too secure because if there is anything that happens it'll be the enemy though just to give everyone here a taste of combat.
>
> Well one good thing here is the price of booze. It seems must be terrible back in the States.
>
> I guess Tricky Dick hasn't ruined the whole country yet. I guess he is having problems and needs a few more years to do an efficient job.
>
> Say hi to the Mothers for Peace for me.
>
> Charlie

## Mothers for Peace — Dorothy's political affiliation

**"Say hi to the Mothers for Peace for me."** The phrasing is casual but the affiliation is concrete. **Mothers for Peace** was a category of organized American anti-Vietnam War groups active across the late 1960s and early 1970s. Many local chapters used the name; a national network of American mothers organized against the war emerged in parallel with Vietnam Veterans Against the War and the broader peace movement. Membership was sometimes affiliated with Quaker, Methodist, or Unitarian congregations, sometimes with Women Strike for Peace (founded 1961), sometimes independent.

What this letter records is that **Dorothy Wildermuth — the wife of Air Force Lieutenant Colonel Robert Earl Wildermuth (a Vietnam-era career officer) — was a member of such a group.** The political configuration is striking on a second read. Robert Earl had retired from the Air Force in May 1970 after a Pacific WWII career, the Korean War, and a Pentagon final assignment. His wife, by the end of that year, had joined the women's peace movement, and his future son-in-law (Charlie) was writing her by name to send his greetings to the group.

The Eesley-Wildermuth families' political stance on the war thus appears organized across multiple fronts in this archive:

- Charlie's [October 1970 vote-for-immediate-pull-out letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-10-vote-immediate-pullout/) — the soldier voting against the war from the field.
- [Peggy McMaster Eesley's 10 February 1971 congressional letter](/docs/letters/peggy-to-terrie-1971-02-10/) — the soldier's mother working U.S. Representative Clarence Miller for Charlie's tour situation.
- Don McMaster's offer (per the summer 1970 ["violent campus" letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-monsoon-5th-day-out/)) — Charlie's uncle making himself available for bail if Terrie were arrested at the post-Kent State Maryland protests.
- **This letter to Dorothy** — Charlie writing to his future mother-in-law in her capacity as a Mothers for Peace member.

The two families together were running anti-war activity across the political, congressional, family-protective, and grassroots-organizing channels simultaneously. The letter is the document that names the last of those four channels.

## The "Tricky Dick" line

*"I guess Tricky Dick hasn't ruined the whole country yet. I guess he is having problems and needs a few more years to do an efficient job."* **Tricky Dick** was the standard contemptuous nickname for Richard Nixon used across the American left and anti-war movement. Charlie's line is heavily ironic — *"a few more years to do an efficient job"* meaning a few more years to finish the ruining. The line is the same dry, smart-ass voice that runs across the corpus, now turned on the sitting President in a letter to his future mother-in-law who he knew shared his politics.

## The guard-duty opening

The letter opens on a moment that has Charlie on guard at Da Nang, hoping the generals don't get complacent because if anything happens at all *"it'll be the enemy though just to give everyone here a taste of combat."* The line frames the post-malaria rear-area period of his tour: he is in the rear, on light guard duty, hoping the war doesn't catch him there.
