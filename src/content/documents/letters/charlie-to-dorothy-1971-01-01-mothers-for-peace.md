## What the letter is

1 January 1971. Charlie writes a short New Year's letter to [Dorothy Hauser](/family/dorothy-hauser/), a family friend of his mother [Peggy McMaster Eesley](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/), from guard duty at Da Nang. Cast confirmed by Chuck Eesley June 2026. (An earlier reading of this letter in this archive — attributing it to Dorothy Marie Davis Wildermuth, Terrie's mother — was retracted in the same conversation; the casual tone, the unguarded *Tricky Dick* line, and the brief *"Charlie"* sign-off all fit the family-friend reading.)

The letter's voice supports the Hauser reading: the casual *"Tricky Dick"* line is the sort of thing a soldier might write a familiar family friend whose politics he knew, but a more formally awkward thing to write his then-girlfriend's mother, the wife of a recently-retired Air Force lieutenant colonel. The brief *"Charlie"* sign-off (not *"Charles"* or *"Charlie Eesley"*) further matches a casual letter between people who knew each other well.

## Transcription

> *Jan 1*
>
> Dear Dorothy,
>
> Happy New Year! It is so hot over here and I'm on guard in Danang. I hope the generals don't feel too secure because if there is anything that happens it'll be the enemy though just to give everyone here a taste of combat.
>
> Well one good thing here is the price of booze. It seems must be terrible back in the States.
>
> I guess Tricky Dick hasn't ruined the whole country yet. I guess he is having problems and needs a few more years to do an efficient job.
>
> Say hi to the Mothers for Peace for me.
>
> Charlie

## Mothers for Peace

The closing line — *"Say hi to the Mothers for Peace for me"* — locates Dorothy Hauser in an organized anti-Vietnam War women's group. *Mothers for Peace* was the name used by several American anti-war mothers' organizations active across the late 1960s and early 1970s. Local chapters varied: some affiliated with Quaker, Methodist, or Unitarian congregations; some with Women Strike for Peace (founded 1961); some independent. The common framing was American mothers organized against the war their sons were fighting.

That a friend of Peggy Eesley's was in such a group — and that Charlie wrote her by name to send his greetings — places the Mothers for Peace network inside Peggy's social circle in Marietta or wherever Dorothy Hauser lived. The Eesley family's broader political stance of the period — Charlie's [October 1970 vote-for-immediate-pull-out letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-10-vote-immediate-pullout/), Peggy's [10 February 1971 congressional letter](/docs/letters/peggy-to-terrie-1971-02-10/), [Uncle Don McMaster's bail-money offer](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-monsoon-5th-day-out/) for Terrie at the post-Kent-State Maryland campus — runs alongside Dorothy Hauser's Mothers for Peace affiliation as part of the same wider anti-war network around the Eesley household.

## The "Tricky Dick" line

*"I guess Tricky Dick hasn't ruined the whole country yet. I guess he is having problems and needs a few more years to do an efficient job."* *Tricky Dick* was the standard contemptuous nickname for Richard Nixon across the American left and anti-war movement. Charlie's line is heavily ironic — *"a few more years to do an efficient job"* meaning a few more years to finish the ruining. The dry, smart-ass voice runs across every Charlie piece in the corpus.

## The guard-duty opening

Charlie is on guard at Da Nang, hoping the generals don't get complacent because if anything happens at all *"it'll be the enemy though just to give everyone here a taste of combat."* The line frames the post-malaria rear-area period of his tour: he is in the rear, on light guard duty, hoping the war doesn't catch him there.

## Why the recipient question matters

The earlier attribution of this letter to Dorothy Wildermuth had supported a richer reading of the Eesley-Wildermuth two-family anti-war network — the soldier's-future-mother-in-law as a fourth organized actor. With the recipient correction, the political picture is somewhat narrower: the anti-war organizing runs through the Eesley side (Peggy and her circle, Don, Charlie himself) rather than across both family lines. Dorothy Wildermuth's actual political stance during this period is open in this archive.
