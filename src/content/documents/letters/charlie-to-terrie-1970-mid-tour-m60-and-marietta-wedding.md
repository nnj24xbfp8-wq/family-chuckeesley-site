## What the letter is

Mid-1970. Charlie writes Terrie from the field after some rough days &mdash; *"no contact"* with the enemy, going through the jungle, ending a three-week dry spell that has just broken with rain. He carries on the same sheet two future-shaping pieces of content: the **M-60 machine gun** rotation he has just been handed, and the first written sentence of the **Marietta wedding planning** that would land thirteen months later.

## Transcription (mid-letter; opening and closing pages not yet sampled)

> *We have had some rough days lately (no contact) going through the jungle. It has been raining lately after about a 3 week dry spell.*
>
> *It should be a near right tonight though. I'm carrying the machine gun now. It weighs 25 lbs and is damn heavy after awhile. I haven't used it and hope I never do.*
>
> *We are not in the [DMZ?] now nor have we been. The only allies there are the S. Vietn., which is where they constantly &hellip; I don't know whether it makes any difference or not though.*
>
> *I guess I'll have to have the other ring cut down to size when I get back. You can plan on Marietta for the wedding if you like. I know my parents want to entertain. You can write any considerate things with them. It can be as large as you can afford.*
>
> *I have Hartz's address &hellip; so I guess I'll write him when I have time. It is getting dark now and I'll write more later.*

## What the letter records

**The M-60 machine gun.** *"I'm carrying the machine gun now. It weighs 25 lbs and is damn heavy after awhile. I haven't used it and hope I never do."* The M-60 was the standard U.S. squad-level **belt-fed 7.62 mm machine gun** of the Vietnam war &mdash; carried by one designated infantry soldier per squad, with the rest of the squad serving as assistant gunners or ammo bearers. The 25-pound weight Charlie names tracks; the gun in its ready-to-fire configuration ran 23&ndash;26 pounds depending on the variant. Being **assigned the M-60** placed Charlie at the **literal spear-tip** of his unit's rotation: the M-60 gunner moved at the center of the squad's fire-team, had to set up the gun whenever the unit took contact, and was a primary target on the other side. The line *"I haven't used it and hope I never do"* is the small honest piece of self-knowledge Charlie consistently writes about his combat role: he wasn't there voluntarily, he wasn't enthusiastic, and he hoped to discharge having never fired it in anger.

The transition months later to the [Da Nang office](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-03-danang-extension/) and the [4 February 1971 Laos fore-warning](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-04-laos-fore-warning/) register made him glad to be one remove from this kind of duty &mdash; *"I'm really glad that I'm out of the field"* &mdash; reads against this mid-1970 letter as the same person, six months later, having succeeded in trading the M-60 carry for a rear-area office.

**The Marietta wedding plan, written from the bush.** *"You can plan on Marietta for the wedding if you like. I know my parents want to entertain. You can write any considerate things with them. It can be as large as you can afford."* This is **the first written reference in the corpus to the Marietta wedding** that would happen in August 1971, after Charlie's [June 14 1971 ETS](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-04-02-parents-against-hawaii/). Will and Peggy Eesley had a 5,000-square-foot designed-and-built home at [Highland Ridge Road](/places/highland-ridge-road/) in Lowell, just outside Marietta, that was built to host extended-family events; their offer to *"entertain"* a Marietta-side wedding was the natural one for Terrie to take up. Charlie's quiet *"as large as you can afford"* defers the scale decision to Terrie and the Wildermuth-side budget, while signalling the Eesley-side stands ready to absorb the host duties. The wedding, when it landed, would happen in this register.

That Charlie wrote both lines &mdash; the M-60 rotation and the wedding plan &mdash; **on the same piece of paper, on the same evening, in the same letter** is what makes the letter remarkable. The two halves of his future are pressed together with no transition.

**The dry spell ending.** *"It has been raining lately after about a 3 week dry spell."* I Corps climate ran wet-and-dry monsoon cycles; a three-week dry spell in the bush meant manageable water-table conditions for foot patrols but also intense heat. The rains breaking it would have produced the standard tropical-storm pattern that the rest of the corpus describes in detail. The detail places the letter in the late-spring / early-summer transition period, July or early August 1970.

**Hartz.** *"I have Hartz's address &hellip; so I guess I'll write him when I have time."* Hartz is most likely a [Marietta High School](/family/charles-eesley/) classmate or NCO school friend Charlie was in touch with by mail. The reference is incidental.
