## What the letter is

21 September 1970. Charlie writes Terrie from a Vietnam rear-area hospital on **American National Red Cross stationery** &mdash; the Red Cross cross logo and the printed line *"THE AMERICAN NATIONAL RED CROSS"* across the top of the cream sheet. He has just been admitted with malaria. He is writing rather than waiting in silence because he hasn't had a letter from her since the week before the 17th and he wants to break the gap.

The Red Cross provided stationery, postage, and writing-assistance services to hospitalized servicemembers in Vietnam through its field offices at base hospitals across the country. A soldier in a hospital bed with no personal stationery could get a pad of Red Cross paper, a pen, and an envelope to write home. The letterhead on this letter is the direct material trace of that service.

## Transcription

> *Sept 21*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> Guess what! Well, here I am in the hospital suffering from Malaria. It is the curable or "nice" receiving kind. My company came in from the field for a week for stand-down and here I sit while they have it easy too. I should be here a couple more weeks. And then I seems back to those damn mountains. It seems the stupid bastards are really hell-bent on keeping me out there in the rain and all. How am I going to take this year? I really hope I get sick more often. Not malaria, just a broken bone or something.
>
> I'm really depressed. The future just looks mighty bleak. I haven't gotten any letter since the week before the 17th so I guess I will have a lot. Right now it is going to take a lot more than letter to keep me going.
>
> Let me tell you some good things. I'm in unaccustomed comfort. I've seen and used flush toilet since I've been here...

## What the letter records

**The "curable or 'nice' receiving kind" of malaria.** Vietnam malaria came in two main forms relevant to American troops: **Plasmodium vivax** (treatable, lower mortality, recurring episodes possible) and **Plasmodium falciparum** (more severe, higher mortality, chloroquine-resistant strains common in Vietnam by the late 1960s). The "curable or nice receiving kind" Charlie names is almost certainly **P. vivax** &mdash; the form that responded to standard chloroquine and primaquine treatment without the life-threatening complications of falciparum. The 101st Airborne operating in the wet mountains of I Corps was a high-risk environment for both, but vivax was the more common diagnosis.

**The standdown irony.** Charlie's company had pulled in from the field for the week's stand-down he himself would otherwise have enjoyed; instead he is in the hospital, watching them have an easier week from a bed. The juxtaposition is the central irony of the letter.

**"I really hope I get sick more often."** The line is the unguarded version of the same wish that runs through the corpus: anything to keep him out of the field. Not malaria again, he qualifies, but a broken bone or something less violent than the field. The hospital was the kind of safety the war kept making impossible to find any other way.

**The mail gap.** *"I haven't gotten any letter since the week before the 17th."* A four-or-five-day mail gap from Maryland to a Vietnam APO was normal; what Charlie is describing is roughly that, complicated by the move from the field to the hospital. The letter is his bridge across the silence rather than a reply to anything.

The page closes mid-sentence on flushed toilets and unaccustomed comfort &mdash; the small luxuries of a rear hospital after months in the field. The 28 September letter from Cam Ranh Bay continues the arc.
