## What the letter is

1 March 1971. Three weeks into [Operation Lam Son 719](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Lam_Son_719), the U.S.-supported ARVN incursion into Laos that Charlie had warned Terrie was coming in his [4 February 1971 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-04-laos-fore-warning/). The campaign was, by 1 March, already in the deteriorating phase that would close it on 25 March with substantial ARVN casualties and a withdrawal that the public reading framed as a setback for Vietnamization.

Charlie was in Da Nang, in an air-conditioned office, with most of his unit moved up to Quang Tri. He had been left behind to pull CQ (Charge of Quarters) &mdash; an administrative night-watch duty &mdash; while the campaign ran its course up north. The slack day let him sit down at a typewriter. The result is the longest typed letter in the corpus and the most sustained piece of Vietnam-war-commentary in Charlie's own voice.

## Transcription

> *1 March 1971*
>
> Dear Terrie,
>
> Most everyone here is or has moved up to Quang Tri and things are very quiet around here. If everyone goes it seems that I will be left behind to pull CQ here in this office. I wouldn't be working very hard but I would have to be here. If the mission up north takes as long as it seems it might to be, I may not be able to make it to Hawaii. It seems that the damn army is going for broke on this campaign that is now in progress. A lot of guys are going to get killed. I'm really glad that I'm out of the field. I plan to sign up for R&R for Hawaii anyway on the dates you want but right now the chances don't seem too good. I doubt if I will get a chance to go on R&R at all. I know I could insist but that really wouldn't be right. If Hawaii doesn't work out maybe when I get back we could just go some place for a week or so. I kind of like the idea of going to Cape Hatteras (sp) North Carolina. I should be able to afford that very easily if I can't go on R&R.
>
> What is new? Did you ever get your grade changed in your Shakespere course? It is really starting to get hot again over here. The rain has stopped. I guess the guys down in Da Nang should be coming back up to their companies pretty soon. I really feel sorry for the guys going back out to the field. This office is airconditioned so actually I don't have it bad at all. Today has been a very slack day which is why I can type this letter. They have a library here too which isn't too bad and I can get all the magazines I want. It takes quite a while for letters to reach here but I guess I will survive. There is nothing I need now except letters so save your money. I am bored but I will be able to survive with just 105 more days to go. It has been a long year. I still have a fairly long way to go but at least the end is in sight. Well I can't think of anything else to say now so I guess I'll close.
>
> Love,
> Charles

## What the letter records

**The Lam Son 719 commentary.** *"It seems that the damn army is going for broke on this campaign that is now in progress. A lot of guys are going to get killed. I'm really glad that I'm out of the field."* The 4 February letter had warned the operation was coming. This letter, three weeks in, reports the operation's character: going for broke, with high casualties on the ARVN side that Charlie could see being reflected in U.S. air-support requests, ammunition deliveries, and medevac routings through Da Nang. The line *"I'm really glad that I'm out of the field"* is the small honest piece of self-knowledge Charlie consistently writes; he was a draftee who had not gone to war voluntarily, and he was relieved to be in a rear office rather than at the spear-tip of a campaign that was going poorly.

The campaign would close on 25 March 1971 with substantial ARVN losses (the public-record range is roughly 1,500–7,500 ARVN dead) and a U.S.-public reading that the incursion had failed to meet its operational objectives. Charlie's 1 March reading &mdash; that the army was going for broke and that a lot of guys would die &mdash; was accurate.

**The R&R question, now nearly closed.** *"I plan to sign up for R&R for Hawaii anyway on the dates you want but right now the chances don't seem too good. I doubt if I will get a chance to go on R&R at all."* The R&R that had been the family-and-propriety crisis of [March-April 1970](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-04-02-parents-against-hawaii/), and that had eventually happened in mid-June 1970, was being planned as a second R&R because of his ETS extension. By 1 March 1971, with the Laos campaign drawing resources, the chances of a second R&R clearing the army's leave system were dropping. Charlie's *"I know I could insist but that really wouldn't be right"* &mdash; the moral note from a soldier who could have pulled the rank-and-procedure strings to push his leave through but chose not to while his unit was running an operation &mdash; is one of the small ethical moments of the corpus.

**Cape Hatteras, North Carolina.** *"If Hawaii doesn't work out maybe when I get back we could just go some place for a week or so. I kind of like the idea of going to Cape Hatteras (sp) North Carolina."* The proposed civilian post-tour Cape-Hatteras week is the lowest-stakes destination in the entire R&R correspondence &mdash; a state-park-and-beach drive of about ten hours from Maryland, no military paperwork required, no parental-propriety problem because by then they would either be married or about to be. The *"(sp)"* parenthetical &mdash; Charlie checking his own spelling of Hatteras in the middle of typing a serious letter &mdash; is one of the small careful notes that runs through his voice across the corpus.

**Terrie's Shakespeare course.** *"Did you ever get your grade changed in your Shakespere course?"* The reference is to Terrie's University of Maryland Shakespeare course of the previous semester &mdash; presumably the same incomplete he had asked her about in the [15 March 1970 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-03-15/). Whether she ever finished the Shakespeare course's missing work, and whether the incomplete became a real letter grade, is an open question in this archive.

**The air-conditioned office.** The small register the letter sits in &mdash; the typed font, the multiple paragraphs, the longer-and-more-thought-out sentences than the handwritten letters &mdash; is the register of someone with time on his hands, a typewriter, and a library down the hall. *"This office is airconditioned so actually I don't have it bad at all. Today has been a very slack day which is why I can type this letter."* The contrast with the field is explicit. The remove from the field is what the letter is finally about.

**105 more days to go.** The countdown thread continues. By his 4 February calculation he had 150 days; on 1 March he reports 105. The 14 June ETS target holds.
