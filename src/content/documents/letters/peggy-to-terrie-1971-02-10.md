## What the letter is

Wednesday 10 February 1971. Charlie's mother [Peggy McMaster Eesley](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) writes from Marietta to Terrie at the University of Maryland. The letter is one of the only surviving pieces of correspondence in this archive between Charlie's mother and Terrie before they were officially family. It carries three threads simultaneously, all woven through the same six paragraphs.

## Thread one — Charlie's month extension at Da Nang

The first sentence is the news: Charlie has been given a one-month tour extension at *"Da Nang"* &mdash; Peggy's spelling of the big U.S. rear base on the central Vietnamese coast. Cross-referenced against Charlie's own letters of 4 February 1971 and 10 February 1971, both of which open *"I'm still in Danang,"* the extension is not a field-combat extension but a rear-area transition: Charlie had been pulled back from Camp Eagle to Da Nang and was waiting for orders for a clerk's job there for the last of his tour.

Peggy's tone in the opening line &mdash; *"By now you of course know that Charlie has a month's extension at Da Nang. Isn't that great?!"* &mdash; is still sardonic. She is writing to the young woman who has spent two years now waiting for Charlie to come home, and any extension &mdash; rear-area or not &mdash; is one more month of waiting.

## Thread two — the CO at Camp Eagle to Congressman Miller

The second thread is the major archive find:

> *"I'm enclosing the final letter from the CO at Camp Eagle to Congressman Miller. I sent it to Charles who commented on it & returned it. When you've examined it would you please return it & I'll add it to all the other stuff I have that pertains to him & this army 'experience'."*

This sentence reveals an entire previously-undocumented layer of the Eesley family's work on Charlie's tour. The 101st Airborne's main base camp was **Camp Eagle**, near Hue. The CO at Camp Eagle is Charlie's commanding officer's command. **Congressman Miller** is the Eesley family's Marietta-area U.S. Representative — almost certainly **Clarence E. Miller (R-OH)**, who represented Ohio's 10th Congressional District (which included Washington County) from January 1967 until 1993.

The implication is that the Eesley family had **worked Congressional channels** about Charlie's tour situation — almost certainly the extension, the assignment, or the conditions — and a formal response came back from the commanding officer at Camp Eagle through Congressman Miller. Peggy mailed the response to Charlie in Vietnam, he commented on it and sent it back, and now Peggy is sending it to Terrie to read and return. She is collecting *"all the other stuff I have that pertains to him & this army 'experience'"* — a personal dossier of the family's working-the-war files.

The letter itself — the CO at Camp Eagle's reply to Congressman Miller — is not in this archive. The 10 February letter records its existence and the circuit it travelled: Camp Eagle → Congressman Miller → Peggy in Marietta → Charlie in Vietnam → back to Peggy → to Terrie in Maryland → back to Peggy.

## Thread three — the stainless steel pattern

The third thread is domestic in the older sense:

> *"Charles tells me that he has sent some stainless steel to you. What's it like — do you know the pattern — hope you like it."*

The Eesleys had picked or commissioned a stainless steel flatware pattern as a gift for Terrie — a pre-engagement gesture and the kind of small material exchange a pre-1970s American family made when a son's relationship was approaching marriage. The detail places this letter exactly where it sits in the timeline: deep enough into the relationship that Charlie's mother is mailing flatware to Terrie at her college dorm.

## Why this letter is structurally important

Most of the corpus is Charlie's voice. This is **Peggy's voice**, addressed to **Terrie**, on **Charlie's tour situation**. It is the only direct primary-source documentation of the **Eesley family's work on Charlie's behalf during the war** — and the only piece of correspondence in the archive between the two women who would eventually be mother-in-law and daughter-in-law.

The Congressional-channel thread sits alongside the closing arc of the [October 1970 "I will vote for the immediate pull-out candidates" letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-10-vote-immediate-pullout/) as evidence that **the Eesley family's political position on Vietnam was active, not passive** — Charlie was voting absentee for pull-out candidates; his mother was writing to Congressman Miller about his tour.
