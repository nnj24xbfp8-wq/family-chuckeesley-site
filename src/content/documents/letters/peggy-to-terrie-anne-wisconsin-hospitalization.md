## What the letter is

Sometime in the mid-1970s through mid-1980s. Peggy McMaster Eesley writes Terrie a *"quickie"* &mdash; her own word for it &mdash; from Highland Ridge in Lowell, Ohio, with the Highland Ridge guest-room-clearing-out project still in process. The trigger for the letter is news that has just come in: [Anne Eesley](/family/anne-eesley/) &mdash; Peggy's middle daughter, Charlie's sister &mdash; has been hospitalized in Wisconsin.

The letter is short, hand-written, and structurally three pieces: the Anne news up front, the pillowcases-apology and the bed-room conversion at Highland Ridge in the middle, and a quiet line about Peggy's own mother-in-law &mdash; [Stella Anderson Eesley](/family/stella-anderson-eesley/) &mdash; that reads as Peggy addressing something more layered.

## Transcription

> *Tuesday Eve 8:30*
>
> Dearest Terrie,
>
> This will be a quickie. We heard Sunday night that Peg Eesley was called to Wisconsin because Anne had a nervous breakdown and was in the hospital, so I thought at first I would call you then thought I had better wait until I knew more. So heard today Peg was back home late last night and a good friend had called and Peg didn't want to talk about it, but did say Anne was not as bad as they first thought &mdash; just exhaustion.
>
> [&hellip;]
>
> [&hellip; that you are not unique in experiencing unhappiness somehow helps.] I liked (and respected) my mother-in-law very much.
>
> In a lighter vein, I was appalled to discover that your bed didn't have any pillowslips with it. I had made those beds up, thinking I'd remember to put pillowslips on before anyone slept in that room again. My apology.
>
> After much tugging, repacking etc., that downstairs room looks like a bed-room now rather than a storage room. Tweren't easy.
>
> With care,
> Peggy Eesley
>
> P.S. Joe, Anne & Stephie will be down for Memorial Day &mdash; and they hope to stay over for a few days.

## What the letter records

**The opening register is third-person.** *"We heard Sunday night that Peg Eesley was called to Wisconsin..."* The third-person register that opens the letter is a small writing-quirk of the Eesley women of that generation &mdash; Peggy describing her own activity in the third person, by her family name, as if she were a friend of the household reporting on Peg. The signature *"Peggy Eesley"* at the close confirms the author. The third-person opening is the letter-writing equivalent of *"we heard at our house..."* &mdash; placing the news at one distance from the reader before bringing it closer.

**Anne's Wisconsin hospitalization.** [Anne Eesley](/family/anne-eesley/) was in her [University of Wisconsin Spanish MA program](/family/anne-eesley/) from approximately 1974 through 1976 &mdash; the period [her obituary](/family/anne-eesley/) places her at Wisconsin. The episode this letter records &mdash; first reported to Peggy as a *"nervous breakdown,"* then revised to *"just exhaustion"* &mdash; would have come during that period, in the demanding final years of her graduate program. Anne's biography, which the [obituary](/family/anne-eesley/) frames around her academic career, did not previously carry this episode; it should now. The detail that Peggy *"didn't want to talk about it"* and that the news came from a good friend who had called is the small private register of how the Eesley women shared serious health news: discretion first, then verified facts.

**The mother-in-law line.** *"...that you are not unique in experiencing unhappiness somehow helps. I liked (and respected) my mother-in-law very much."* The parenthetical *"(and respected)"* is the careful note: Peggy did not just like [Stella Anderson Eesley](/family/stella-anderson-eesley/), she respected her, and the distinction is meaningful. Stella had been the [wartime mother who held the family together through Lyle's Bataan death](/family/stella-anderson-eesley/); Peggy's read of her mother-in-law as someone to respect rather than merely tolerate was not trivial.

That Peggy was sharing this with Terrie, in a letter that opens with her own daughter's hospitalization and is otherwise about Highland Ridge housekeeping, reads as Peggy doing something quiet and deliberate. The line lands inside the broader context of a daughter-in-law / mother-in-law relationship in which Peggy was the mother-in-law. The line is Peggy offering Terrie the small piece of testimony that this relationship can be one of liking and respect, even when its register has been hard. *"That you are not unique in experiencing unhappiness somehow helps"* is the bridging line that frames it: Peggy is acknowledging Terrie's unhappiness and offering, by way of comfort, the example of her own positive experience with the same role.

For the family archive, the line is a small piece of testimony that the Peggy &mdash; Terrie relationship had its own register of difficulty, and that Peggy was, in writing, doing what she could to bridge it.

**The pillowcases and the downstairs bedroom.** *"I was appalled to discover that your bed didn't have any pillowslips with it. I had made those beds up, thinking I'd remember to put pillowslips on before anyone slept in that room again. My apology. After much tugging, repacking etc., that downstairs room looks like a bed-room now rather than a storage room. Tweren't easy."* The small piece of [Highland Ridge](/places/highland-ridge-road/) operations: Peggy had been treating the downstairs guest room as a storage room when Will (or Will and Peggy together, before his 1986 death) didn't need it for visitors, and had recently cleared it out for an upcoming Terrie-and-Charlie visit. The pillowcases she had forgotten to put on the bed; the rest of the room she had reclaimed from storage with effort. The dialectical *"Tweren't easy"* is Peggy's quiet Marietta-vernacular sign-off &mdash; the small piece of speech-register that survived from the McMaster-side East Tennessee Anderson grandparent's voice into the third generation.

**The Memorial Day P.S.** *"Joe, Anne & Stephie will be down for Memorial Day &mdash; and they hope to stay over for a few days."* This narrows the dating window: Joe is [Joseph Wetters](/family/joseph-wetters/), Anne's eventual chosen-family partner; Stephie is Joe's daughter Stephanie. The reference to the three of them as a unit places the letter **after Anne and Joe had become a household together**, which the [Anne Eesley obituary](/family/anne-eesley/) places in the late 1970s through the 1980s. Anne's Wisconsin MA was 1974-76, but she continued at Wisconsin briefly afterward, and Joe was in her life by the late 1970s.

## Where this fits in the archive

The Anne hospitalization belongs on [Anne's page](/family/anne-eesley/) as the **small documented health episode during her Wisconsin years** &mdash; an episode the [obituary's professional-career frame](/family/anne-eesley/) had not preserved. *"Nervous breakdown"* was the period word for what the doctors revised down to *"exhaustion"* &mdash; an emotionally and physically demanding period of graduate-school work that produced an acute episode but resolved without lasting damage. The episode does not change the trajectory of Anne's career; it adds a small piece of the human texture of how that trajectory was lived.

The mother-in-law line belongs on [Peggy's page](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) and on [Stella Anderson Eesley's page](/family/stella-anderson-eesley/) as **a small piece of testimony from one McMaster-side daughter-in-law about her predecessor in the same role**. Peggy liked and respected Stella. Stella's life on this site has been threaded mostly through wartime &mdash; the [Lyle POW death](/family/lyle-eesley/), the [c. 1937-39 group portrait](/archive/eesley-children-portrait-c-1937-39/) of the six surviving Charles Leonard children including her own, the [1899 family group](/archive/eesley-family-group-portrait-1899/) of her in-laws. The line *"liked (and respected)"* adds the small piece of the relationship-register, from one of the people who knew her best.
