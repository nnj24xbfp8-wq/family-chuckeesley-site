## What the letter is

Late 1970 or very early 1971. Charlie writes Terrie what is, by any measure, the most candid letter the Vietnam corpus carries. The date isn't on the surviving sheet, but the reference to Christmas and New Year's flares as a recent comparison &mdash; *"it should get pretty noisy around &hellip; til as it was noisy on Xmas and New Year's. They lit up the sky with flares"* &mdash; places the letter in the first half of January 1971, in the same Da Nang-rear-area period that produced the [19 January ETS-extension letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-01-19-ets-extension/), the [29 January monsoon letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-marijuana-and-donut-dollies/) and the early-February Laos letters.

It is Charlie writing about the culture he was inside &mdash; not the war as policy, and not his own days, but **the social fabric of soldiers in I Corps at the end of 1970**.

## Transcription

> *&hellip; a lot of flares &mdash; It should get pretty noisy around &hellip; til as it was noisy on Xmas and New Year's. They lit up the sky with flares.*
>
> *I have only tried a few puffs of marijuana back at Evans and I was drunk at the time. I really don't go for it and neither do most around me, but it is wide spread as are other drugs. The kids will try to sell it to us through the fence along with anything else they have to sell. Also they want to buy our cigarettes as it costs us 17¢ pack and theirs around .95.*
>
> *As far as your thinking marijuana is dangerous, I don't think so at all. I just hate smoking of all kinds and the army is cracking down on it and I don't want any more trouble besides.*
>
> *If you tell your medic friend I said [&hellip;]. Most everyone I know (married or not) [&hellip;]. Well believe it or not I'm still a good boy as far as &hellip; relations are concerned.*
>
> *[Next sheet]*
>
> *I hope you don't think I'm comparing you with anyone over here, though.*
>
> *There are some American Red Cross girls over here and are called "Donut Dollies"! Some are pretty sad looking even for [the GIs] and we fondly call them "Biscuit Bitches," although not to their face. Just thought you would like to know a little of what goes on. I'll be telling you for the rest of your life so don't get too curious.*
>
> *I'll write again tomorrow night &mdash; esp. if I receive another letter from you.*
>
> *Love,*
> *Charles*

## What the letter records

**The Camp Evans flare register.** *"It should get pretty noisy around &hellip; til as it was noisy on Xmas and New Year's. They lit up the sky with flares."* American firebases in I Corps in 1970 routinely fired illumination flares to mark Christmas and New Year's &mdash; partly as morale, partly to draw out any enemy probing actions taking advantage of the holiday distraction. Charlie's casual reference *"it was noisy"* is the small note of a soldier who had lived through both holidays in country and had heard the flares overhead from a rear position. By the [late January 1971 letters](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-01-19-ets-extension/) he was thinking about how long he could stretch the rear-area assignment; this letter is the same person, slightly earlier, in the same place.

**Marijuana through the firebase wire.** *"The kids will try to sell it to us through the fence along with anything else they have to sell. Also they want to buy our cigarettes as it costs us 17¢ pack and theirs around .95."* The economic detail is exact and small. American cigarettes sold to GIs at PX prices &mdash; *17 cents a pack* &mdash; were a hard currency in country, where Vietnamese cigarettes ran several times that. Vietnamese kids near firebases ran the trade in both directions: cigarettes one way, marijuana and other goods the other. The image of *"selling it to us through the fence"* is the literal infrastructure of how American drug use in Vietnam scaled &mdash; not back-channel networks, but a transactional ecosystem at every wire perimeter.

Charlie's read &mdash; *"I have only tried a few puffs of marijuana back at Evans and I was drunk at the time. I really don't go for it and neither do most around me, but it is wide spread"* &mdash; is one of the more honest sentences in the corpus. He was not an enthusiast. He was not a denouncer. He was a draftee in a war culture where drug use was, by 1970, broadly normalized, and he was registering that fact while keeping himself on the safe side of the line.

**The argument with Terrie about marijuana.** *"As far as your thinking marijuana is dangerous, I don't think so at all. I just hate smoking of all kinds and the army is cracking down on it and I don't want any more trouble besides."* Terrie's prior letter had apparently expressed concern about the danger of marijuana. Charlie's response disagrees on the substance &mdash; he didn't think the drug was dangerous &mdash; but agrees on the conclusion: he would stay away from it anyway. The deciding factor was the army's enforcement crackdown, not the drug's effects.

**The Red Cross "Donut Dollies."** The American Red Cross's [SRAO program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donut_Dollies) (Supplemental Recreational Activities Overseas) sent young American women to South Vietnam from 1965 to 1972 as morale officers to American troops. They visited firebases and rear-area units, ran activities, served coffee and donuts, and gave the men a few hours of conversation with women from home. By 1970 the program had become culturally complicated &mdash; the women were treated by some soldiers as morale touchstones and by others through the lens of the broader sexual culture surrounding the war. The nickname *"Biscuit Bitches"* that Charlie reports was period soldier slang &mdash; *"although not to their face"* &mdash; and the line *"some are pretty sad looking even for [the GIs]"* is the bleak, joke-form register Charlie sometimes used about the culture he was watching.

**The self-positioning.** *"Well believe it or not I'm still a good boy as far as &hellip; relations are concerned."* The line is Charlie locating himself relative to the surrounding GI culture &mdash; not in it, on its edge, in private correspondence with the woman he intended to marry. It is one of the small private notes the corpus carries: a young man on a year-long tour at twenty-three, in a war that had reshaped much of his cohort, writing to his fiancée to assure her that the rear-area Da Nang office hadn't yet reshaped him.

## Editorial note

This letter, more than any other in the corpus, was written privately and frankly. Following the [editorial principle for intimate-register lines in family letters](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-05-29/), the explicit lines in the sexual-register passage have been elided with `[&hellip;]` while the substantive content &mdash; what Charlie was observing about the culture around him, how he was reading it, and where he was positioning himself in it &mdash; has been kept. The candor of the letter is part of why it matters: this is the only letter in the corpus where Charlie writes at length about the social fabric of GIs in Vietnam, the drug economy at the wire, and the Red Cross presence. The substance of what he was watching is the historical record. The privacy of the lines between him and Terrie remains theirs.
