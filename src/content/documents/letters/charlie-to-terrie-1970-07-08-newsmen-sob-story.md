## What the letter is

A multi-day letter running roughly 8-12 July 1970 &mdash; Charlie at the two-month mark in country, in a field rotation he is growing to hate, with three substantive pieces of content that the rest of the corpus does not carry.

## Transcription (extracted from the surviving sheets)

> *&hellip; for several months so don't stay up waiting.*
>
> *It seems that I will be spending most of my time in the field (which I'm growing to hate) so if you send anything keep it small. Also just send Kool-Aid in your letters or something. It doesn't have to be much. Just keep writing letters &mdash; I got 5 last time. I don't really need anything. They do provide the basics here.*
>
> *None of my letters or your letters are censored so you can say anything you want. By the time you get this letter I'll have 2 months done over here. 12 July. It seems short and it seems like a lifetime. I do hope you can graduate next spring. I know I won't be able to settle down and work after I get back. I just want to travel and bum around.*
>
> *I can't think of much to say and I'll finish this letter latter.*
>
> *8 July 70. Another day passes. I'm glad to hear &hellip; [continuation through a page not yet sampled in this archive] &hellip;*
>
> *&hellip; you are leaving here, going to the beach etc.*
>
> *We are going to get 4 newsmen out here for 2 days soon. I'll give one of them my sob story and see if I can't sway public opinion.*
>
> *I have very little time now and will try to write more next time.*
>
> *Love,*
> *Charles*

## What the letter records

**The censorship discovery.** *"None of my letters or your letters are censored so you can say anything you want."* This is the small administrative detail that shapes the rest of the corpus's tone, in retrospect. Soldiers had reasonably expected outgoing mail to be censored, as had been the practice in earlier wars; Vietnam-tour outgoing mail was largely not, as Charlie discovers in July 1970. After this letter, he writes more candidly &mdash; the [marijuana-and-Donut-Dollies letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-marijuana-and-donut-dollies/), the [4 February 1971 Laos fore-warning](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-04-laos-fore-warning/), the [1 March 1971 Lam Son 719 commentary](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-03-01-lam-son-719/) all sit on the other side of this discovery. He knew the army wasn't reading his mail.

**The post-tour premonition.** *"I know I won't be able to settle down and work after I get back. I just want to travel and bum around."* The earliest version, by date, of the post-Vietnam-Charlie that Chuck would later [eulogize](/docs/eulogy-charles-mcmaster-eesley/). Charlie was twenty-three, two months into his tour, projecting forward to a self that would come back unable to slide into the standard Marietta-college-grad professional track. *"Travel and bum around"* was the form the projection took in his own voice. Whether the projection held when the [June 14 1971 ETS](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-04-02-parents-against-hawaii/) actually arrived &mdash; the marriage to Terrie [in August 1971](/family/charles-eesley/), the move into Marietta-based stockbroker work &mdash; is the rest of the story. But in July 1970 he was already telling Terrie what he thought the tour was doing to him.

**The newsmen letter &mdash; *"sway public opinion."*** *"We are going to get 4 newsmen out here for 2 days soon. I'll give one of them my sob story and see if I can't sway public opinion."* This is the only letter in the corpus where Charlie writes about **deliberately working the press**. Late-1970 was the peak of correspondent saturation in I Corps &mdash; American newspapers and television networks had reporters embedded with units across the area, looking for soldier-voice stories that fit the war's late-Vietnamization-era public reading. Charlie was reading that situation accurately and planning his own insertion into it: one of the four visiting newsmen would get his individual *"sob story"* &mdash; whichever Charlie thought would land &mdash; and the line of public-opinion influence would run from his field-grade life back through the press to American living rooms.

Whether any actual story made it into print is unrecorded in the corpus. What is recorded is the **strategic instinct**: a 23-year-old draftee in his second month of a Vietnam tour, conscious enough of the press cycle to plan a story-placement at the individual-soldier level. The line sits at the same register as the [populist 'screw the gov't' postage line of the 18 July 1970 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-07-18-no-mail/) ten days later &mdash; both moments of Charlie reading the broader system of the war and looking for the small piece of it he could push back at.

**The Kool-Aid request.** *"Just send Kool-Aid in your letters or something. It doesn't have to be much."* The small practical-care request that runs through the corpus; the Kool-Aid mix-in-water packets were a way to make the tepid jungle water at field bases tolerable. The packages backlog that the [18 July letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-07-18-no-mail/) would name a week and a half later was already accumulating by July 8.
