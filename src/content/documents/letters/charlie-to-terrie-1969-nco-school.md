## What the letter is

**Late 1969.** Charlie is in **U.S. Army NCO school** stateside — most likely **Fort Benning, Georgia**, the standard NCOC (Noncommissioned Officer Candidate) site through this period. He has finished his Marietta College economics degree, taken his commission path through Officer Candidate School and Non-Commissioned Officer Candidate School, and is in the back-half of the training pipeline that ends with deployment to Vietnam. He hasn't shipped yet. He is, in this letter, complaining about a Saturday inspection.

The letter is the **earliest layer of the Vietnam-correspondence voice**: the dry observation, the contempt for petty regulation, the strategic decision to keep his own counsel about what assignment he will and will not accept. *"I'm really beginning to hate the army. It is all so petty."* That sentence — slightly compressed — recurs through the war letters.

## Why this letter is private

The body is **withheld at the family's request**. The page is catalogued here so the corpus arc is complete: the predeployment stateside frustrations as the necessary prologue to the in-country letters that begin in late 1969. The contents are kept in the family archive only.

## What the metadata records

- **Where he was**: Army NCO school in the U.S., presumed Fort Benning.
- **Where he was writing to**: Terrie at the University of Maryland.
- **What was on his mind**: Saturday inspection prep; another company's graduation and assignment to Vietnam; his decision to refuse NCO school placement and aim for D.C. instead; his observation that "I'm really beginning to hate the army."
- **What it tells us about the chronology**: A graduating NCO school company has been sent to Vietnam ahead of his own — placing the letter at a moment when Charlie still has the option to refuse NCO placement and try to stay stateside. By the time of the [22 June 1970 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-june-22/) he is in country with the 101st. Somewhere between this letter and that one, the decision was no longer his.
