## What this document is

On **1 January 1990**, Chuck's maternal grandfather Robert Earl Wildermuth &mdash; then sixty-five, retired in Orlando, eight years past finishing the [WWII combat log](/docs/the-big-one/) and the [autobiography](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/) of his own life &mdash; printed and bound *The Wildermuth/Fleming Heritage*. It was the culmination of **eighteen years of genealogical research** he had begun in **late 1971** at age forty-six.

The document opens with the epigraph he had carried with him throughout the research:

> *Can one tell where he's going until he knows where he's been?*

It runs **24 pages** in three sections: (1) **Wildermuth research narrative** through the German origins, the 1847 emigration, and the early Ohio years; (2) **Fleming research narrative** tracing his mother's Welsh-Irish line back to John Fleming of Montgomery County, Maryland in the 1750s; (3) **Dorothy Marie Davis's family** with her birth certificate and childhood photographs.

It is dedicated to his parents [Sadye Irene Fleming](/family/sadye-fleming-wildermuth/) and [Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/), and to his wife [Dorothy Marie Davis](/family/dorothy-davis-wildermuth/), who *"spent many long hours waiting for me to finish my poring over old court records and documents in my quest for my 'roots'."*

## The findings — what was new when he printed it, and what this archive draws from it

### The Wildermuth origin in Württemberg

> *As Dr. Hans Wildermuth, son of Johann David Wildermuth, stated: "The Wuerttemburg Wildermuths have their origin in the villages of Pleidelsheim and Riedenshausen [Rielingshausen] near the small town of Marbach in the Northeast of Stuttgart."*

Robert Earl wrote to the **burgermeisters of [Marbach](/places/marbach-wildermuthstrasse/), Pleidelsheim, and [Rielingshausen](/places/rielingshausen-church/)** asking for records of a seventeen-year-old Johann Michael who may have left their city for the United States in 1847. **All three responses came back negative.** Then he turned to the **Latter Day Saints records in Salt Lake City** and found Wildermuths in a fourth nearby village, **[Grossaspach](/places/grossaspach/)**. He wrote to the mayor of Grossaspach in turn; the mayor passed the letter to a city genealogist; the genealogist checked the parish records and confirmed that **Johann Michael Wildermuth had in fact been born in Grossaspach.**

Going back three more generations from Johann Michael, Robert Earl reached **the deepest documented Wildermuth ancestor in this archive: [Andreus Wildermuth](/family/andreus-wildermuth/), born around 1748 in Rielingshausen.** Andreus was a **Gartner (vineyard gardener)**. He was the father of [Adam Wildermuth](/family/adam-wildermuth/) (born 1768 Rielingshausen).

### The 1847 emigration of Johann Michael Wildermuth

In the **1860 census of the 3rd Ward of Marietta City, Washington County, Ohio,** Robert Earl found a **John M. Wildermuth, age 29, a shoemaker from Wuerttemburg, Germany,** living in the household of **William Rossar**, also a shoemaker, age 38, from the Duchy of Baden, Germany. Rossar's wife Frederika was from Wuerttemburg &mdash; possibly Johann M.'s sister.

Tracking through the **Passenger and Immigration Lists Index** (Philby & Meyer), Robert Earl found that **Johann Michael Wildermuth and the Rossars had traveled together** to America. Initial research suggested Philadelphia in 1847, but **after three years of correspondence with the Philadelphia city archivist, an unexpected letter in August 1986 informed him that the naturalization record had actually been filed in New York City** &mdash; meaning Johann Michael had landed at the Port of New York, not Philadelphia.

### The Fleming line back to colonial Maryland

For his mother Sadye Irene Fleming's side, Robert Earl traced the Flemings from her birth in **Waverly, Pleasant County, West Virginia** &mdash; though no birth certificate was ever filed; *"travel throughout rural West Virginia in 1900 was extremely difficult..., the lack of adequate roads, the fording of countless streams..., word of a birth was primarily spread by word of mouth with no one taking official notice"* &mdash; back through the West Virginia census records to **John Fleming of Montgomery County, Maryland in the 1750s**.

The earliest Fleming material came from his **pen-pal Michael G. Roberts, 682 Springwater Drive, Akron, Ohio 44313**, a fellow Fleming genealogist Robert Earl never met in person. *"I have closely checked all of his information,"* Robert Earl wrote, *"and except for the John Fleming who came from Ireland (he was Scotch however &mdash; Scotch Irish or a Scot living in Northern Ireland) I have verified all of the information."*

### The Davis family in Waterford, Ohio

The Davis section is centered on Robert Earl's wife Dorothy Marie Davis's birth and childhood. The Ohio Department of Health birth certificate is reproduced in the document:

- **Dorothy Marie Davis** &mdash; born **11:15 AM, 24 February 1925**, in **Waterford Township, Washington County, Ohio**.
- **Father**: **[Homer Davis](/family/homer-davis/)**, age 25, white, born in Ohio, **truck driver** of Waterford.
- **Mother**: **[Bessie M. (Hill) Davis](/family/bessie-hill-davis/)**, age 19, white, born in Ohio, housewife of Waterford. Maiden name **Hill**.
- **One other child** of Bessie was already living at the time (Dorothy's older sister Mary, age ~2).

The document includes **photographs of the Waterford family home** (where Dorothy was born; later destroyed in a fire), **the three Davis sisters as small children** (Mary, Dorothy, and Betty &mdash; ages 5, 4, and 3 in one frame; 6, 5, and 4 with puppies and bare feet in another), **two formal portraits of Dorothy at ages 18 and 19** (1943 and 1944), and a **1968 family photograph** of Jean, Robert Earl Sr., Dorothy Marie, and Robert Earl Jr. ("Bobby").

The fire that destroyed the family home is described:

> *In Unionville [Ohio], while the parents were away from home, they suffered a tremendous calamity. The house caught fire and burned to the ground. This was a real tragedy but with the help of friends and neighbors, they recovered, moved into a small shed not damaged in the fire while their parents lived in a poorly insulated one-room garage throughout the cold winter that followed.*

Mary, one year older than Dorothy, and Dorothy started their education at **Unionville Grade School**. They later transferred to **Marietta High School**; Dorothy graduated in **June 1943**.

### Notable side-discovery: Ottilie Wildermuth the German poetess

Robert Earl noted along the way that there is a **Wildermuth Strasse in Tübingen, Germany** &mdash; not far from the Stuttgart-area Wildermuth villages. The street is named for **[Ottilie Wildermuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottilie_Wildermuth)** (1817&ndash;1877), a well-known 19th-century German poetess, wife of Dr. Johann David Wildermuth, a German high school professor. Whether she is in the same Württemberg Wildermuth line as Robert Earl's branch is not established; he notes only the namesake street. The archive's existing person page for an Ottilie Wildermuth (Chuck's maternal-side ancestor) is a different Ottilie &mdash; the convergence of name is its own small note.

## The research method itself

The eighteen-year arc of *how* Robert Earl built this is itself a piece of mid-20th-century American genealogical practice. The institutions and methods he used:

- **The Orlando Public Library** &mdash; he praises its genealogy department: *"the reputation of having one of the finest genealogy departments in the south-east and after having used it for many years and having visited many, many others throughout the eastern United States, I must agree that it is the best."*
- **The Ohio State Library of Congress** in Columbus.
- **The Passenger and Immigration Lists Index, First Edition Volume 4** by F. William Philby and Mary K. Meyer.
- **The Philadelphia Naturalization Records, Book III**.
- **The U.S. Census records** for Washington County, Ohio (1860 et seq.) and West Virginia counties.
- **The Latter Day Saints Library** in Salt Lake City, Utah &mdash; their International Genealogical Index.
- **Letters to German burgermeisters** (mayors) of every plausible Wildermuth village.
- **Letters to the West Virginia county court houses** (Pleasant, Wood, etc.) for Fleming birth and marriage records.
- **Pen-pal correspondence** with other genealogists, especially Michael G. Roberts of Akron, OH on the Fleming line.

His advice in the closing paragraph, addressed to *"any future family genealogists"*:

> *There is still a lot of work to be done; a lot of questions to be answered and a lot of rewarding records to be found by someone who is inquisitive enough and interested enough to dig, dig, dig. HAPPY HUNTING!*

The line is one this archive is built to honor &mdash; Robert Earl's 1990 advice has become the operating principle of the project he never saw.

## Follow-up research, June 2026

The four queued items from the original 1990 closing list have been pulled forward into this June 2026 desk pass. Where each was *resolved,* the finding is recorded below; where the answer requires archival visits or paid-database queries, the route forward is laid out.

### 1. Andreus Wildermuth's earlier generations — partially resolved

Andreus Wildermuth (b. ~1748, Rielingshausen, *Gartner*) remains the deepest documented Wildermuth direct ancestor in this archive. The contextual finding from the June 2026 desk pass is that the [Rielingshausen Wildermuth clan is documented in parish ledgers from 1521](https://www.kultur-und-heimatverein-rielingshausen.de/start/kelter/), and the standing academic study of the family [*Die Wildermuth in Erdmannhausen*](https://regionalia.blb-karlsruhe.de/frontdoor/index/index/docId/22257) (Karlsruhe BLB OPUS) traces the relevant Marbach-district lines forward from the 16th century. One mid-17th-century instance &mdash; *Casper Wildermuth* (1655&ndash;1727) of Rielingshausen, son of *Hans Wildermuth* and *Maria Klinger* &mdash; is fully [profiled on Geni](https://www.geni.com/people/Casper-Wildermuth/346364312150013734) and likely sits two to four generations above Andreus through the same village. The path forward is a [FamilySearch Württemberg, Germany, Lutheran Baptisms, Marriages, and Burials, 1500-1985](https://www.ancestry.com/search/collections/61023/) query on the Rielingshausen parish books, or a direct request to the Landeskirchliches Archiv Stuttgart for the *Kirchenbuch* of Rielingshausen 1700–1800. Queued, not closed.

### 2. The 1847 New York passenger manifest — route forward identified

Robert Earl's last open Heritage question. The desk pass confirms the route, even if the manifest itself remains to be pulled: the [New York Passenger Lists, 1820&ndash;1891 at FamilySearch](https://www.familysearch.org/search/collection/1849782) covers exactly the relevant period (Castle Garden did not open until 1855, so 1847 New York arrivals went through Battery / Quarantine on Staten Island and are catalogued via the same FamilySearch collection). The [Germans to America, 1840&ndash;1849](https://www.germanroots.com/gtoa2.html) printed series and the [Württemberg Emigration Index](https://www.familysearch.org/en/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg_Emigration_Index_(7_volumes)) (7 vols., Ancestry, 1986–98) are the two specialty finding aids for a 17-year-old Württemberg shoemaker named Johann Michael Wildermuth landing at the Port of New York in 1847. The Württemberg Emigration Index is the most likely place to surface the official Württemberg-side *Auswanderungs-Akte* — the German emigration permit that names the destination port and the year of departure.

### 3. Ottilie Wildermuth — *resolved*

Ottilie Wildermuth (1817&ndash;1877) connects to this branch *by marriage,* not descent &mdash; and her husband's home village turns out to be one of the same three the 1990 Heritage names as the Wildermuth heartland. She married [Wilhelm David Wildermuth](https://www.wildermuth-gymnasium.de/fileadmin/user_upload/Bilder/PDF/Schilling-Wildermuthschule.pdf) (1807&ndash;1885), born in *Pleidelsheim,* son of farmer *Johann Georg Wildermuth*. Pleidelsheim is one of the three Marbach-district villages Robert Earl wrote to in his 1970s burgermeister-correspondence campaign, and is part of the same tight Rielingshausen-clan radius that the 1521 records anchor. So the *"Dr. Hans Wildermuth, son of Johann David Wildermuth"* whom Robert Earl quoted in the epigraph of the Wildermuth section &mdash; *"The Wuerttemburg Wildermuths have their origin in the villages of Pleidelsheim and Riedenshausen [Rielingshausen]"* &mdash; was someone in Ottilie's branch, a son or grandson of Wilhelm David. The two branches are extended cousins through the same surname-cluster. The Tübingen [Wildermuth-Gymnasium](https://www.wildermuth-gymnasium.de) and the Marbach Wildermuthstraße are named for *this* line; the Marietta line of Robert Earl's research is the *other* American twig of the same root. Ottilie's page now carries the resolved branch-relationship.

Eberhard Wildermuth (1890&ndash;1952), her grandson, served as West German Federal Minister for Housing and Construction under Adenauer (1949&ndash;52) &mdash; the public-life continuation of the same Tübingen branch.

### 4. Michael G. Roberts's Fleming research files — open, route documented

The June 2026 web pass did not surface a deposited Fleming archive under Michael G. Roberts's name. His address as of 1990 &mdash; *682 Springwater Drive, Akron, Ohio 44313* &mdash; sits in the West Akron neighborhood served by the [Akron-Summit County Public Library's Special Collections / Genealogy Department](https://www.akronlibrary.org/locations/main-library/special-collections/genealogy/), which is the most plausible Ohio deposit point if the family chose to donate. The route forward: a written inquiry to ASCPL Special Collections; a parallel inquiry to the [Ohio Genealogical Society's Summit County chapter](https://www.ogs.org/); and an obituary-index search via [Akron Beacon Journal Obituary Indexes (1937&ndash;present)](https://www.akronlibrary.org/locations/main-library/special-collections/genealogy/akron-beacon-journal-obituary-indexes) to find when he died and which family members survived him. Queued.
