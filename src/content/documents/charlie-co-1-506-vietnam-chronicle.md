The [letters Charlie Eesley wrote home from Vietnam](/docs/letters-from-vietnam/) in 1970-1971 are personal &mdash; sent to Terrie, to his parents, to family friends. They survive without their original unit context. The unit was **Charlie Company, 1st Battalion, 506th Infantry Regiment, 3rd Brigade, 101st Airborne Division** &mdash; one of the four rifle companies of an infantry battalion that fought in I Corps (northern South Vietnam) from late 1967 through December 1971. This page assembles the unit-history context from the published record so the letters can be read against what the company was doing during the months they were written.

The primary source for the chronicle below is **[William Higgins, *Charlie 1-506th Infantry: Vietnam Chronicle 1967-1971*](https://charlie1-506.com/html/pages%20from%20main%20nav/CreateSpace%20Charlie%20Co.htm)** &mdash; assembled from battalion and brigade journals, after-action reports, morning reports, and first-hand accounts gathered from about fifty of the roughly 1,200 men who served in the company across its four years in Vietnam. Higgins himself served in Charlie Company from July 1969 through July 1970, the year before Charlie Eesley's own tour. The companion site at **[charlie1-506.com](https://charlie1-506.com/index.html)** carries photographs, maps, statistics, and personal recollections from other Charlie Company veterans.

## The unit lineage

The **506th Parachute Infantry Regiment** was activated in July 1942 at Camp Toccoa, Georgia. Its 2nd Battalion's Easy Company became the *Band of Brothers* of Stephen Ambrose's history and the HBO miniseries. The regimental motto &mdash; **"Currahee"** &mdash; is a Cherokee word meaning "we stand alone," taken from Currahee Mountain at the training camp.

The **3rd Brigade of the 101st Airborne Division ("Screaming Eagles")**, with the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd Battalions of the 506th Infantry, deployed to Vietnam in **November-December 1967**. The 1-506th &mdash; "the Currahees" &mdash; was one of three infantry battalions in the brigade (alongside 2-506th Infantry and 3-187th Infantry). Each battalion had four rifle companies (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta) plus headquarters and a support company (Echo, housing recon platoon and mortars). **Charlie Company** &mdash; about 120-150 men at full strength, organized into four platoons &mdash; is the unit in question.

The 3rd Brigade landed at Bien Hoa airbase north of Saigon and was initially based at **Phuoc Vinh** (50 km NNE of Saigon) in III Corps. Through 1968 the brigade fought in the III Corps area &mdash; Phuoc Vinh, Song Be, Cu Chi, Dak To. In **November 1968** the brigade moved north to **Camp Evans** and the 1-506th relocated to **Camp Eagle** in I Corps &mdash; the northern military region of South Vietnam, the area around Hue and Phu Bai. Camp Eagle would be the battalion's principal base for the rest of the war.

## I Corps — the operating area for Charlie Eesley's tour

By the time Charlie Eesley arrived in Vietnam in mid-1970, the company's operating area had been settled for almost two years: the foothills, river valleys, and mountains west of Hue, between **Camp Eagle** (the divisional base near Phu Bai), **Camp Evans** (north of Hue), and the **A Shau Valley** &mdash; the chain of valleys running south from the Demilitarized Zone into Laos that served as the NVA's principal supply corridor in I Corps.

Key firebases of the 1-506th's operational area &mdash; many of which appear in Higgins's chronicle and many of which Charlie's letters allude to:

- **Camp Eagle** &mdash; division base near Phu Bai, the 1-506th's home base. *(Charlie's [10 February 1971 Camp Eagle letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-10-camp-eagle/) was written from here.)*
- **Camp Evans** &mdash; brigade base north of Hue
- **FB Jack, FB Sword, FB Veghel, FB Bastogne, FB Birmingham, FB Tomahawk, FB Ripcord** &mdash; the firebase chain northwest, west, and southwest of Camp Evans
- **FB Currahee, FB Blaze, FB Berchtesgaden, FB Currahee Mountain** &mdash; firebases on Route 547, the road built into the A Shau Valley in 1969
- **Cam Ranh Bay** &mdash; rear-area logistics and convalescent center on the central coast, where Charlie was sent to recover from malaria. *(Charlie's [Cam Ranh Bay letters](/places/cam-ranh-bay/) date from late September 1970.)*
- **Da Nang** &mdash; major US base on the central coast. *(Charlie's [1 January 1971 letter to Dorothy Hauser](/docs/letters/charlie-to-dorothy-hauser-1971-01-01/) was written from here.)*

## What was happening in 1970-1971 — the period of Charlie's tour

Charlie Eesley's tour falls in **the second half of the Vietnamization era** &mdash; the period of phased US troop withdrawal and turnover of combat operations to ARVN. President Nixon had announced the first US troop withdrawals in June 1969. American troop strength in Vietnam fell from a peak of 543,400 in April 1969 to roughly 156,800 by 31 December 1971. The 101st Airborne was one of the last US divisions to depart, with the 3rd Brigade closing out in December 1971.

The major operational events of the 1-506th's 1970-1971 period:

### FB Ripcord — March-July 1970

**Operation Texas Star** (1 April to 5 September 1970) was the 101st Airborne's last large-scale offensive operation of the war. **Firebase Ripcord** &mdash; a mountain firebase about 30 km west of Hue, overlooking the A Shau Valley &mdash; came under sustained siege by NVA regulars from late March through July 1970. The 1-506th participated heavily. On **23 July 1970**, with NVA fire rendering the position untenable, the firebase was evacuated &mdash; the **last major US ground operation of the Vietnam War**, and the last large-scale firebase abandonment under enemy fire. **Lt Col Andre Lucas** of the 2-506th was killed at FB Ripcord on 23 July 1970 and was awarded the Medal of Honor posthumously.

Charlie Eesley's letters home from **July-August 1970** &mdash; the [18 July "screw the gov't anyway you can" postage letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-07-18-no-mail/), the [8 July "newsmen + sob story" letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-07-08-newsmen-sob-story/) &mdash; were written during this window. The 1-506th's tactical situation in those weeks is the unit-history context behind the bleak tone.

### Vietnamization withdrawal — Fall 1970

By autumn 1970 the unit-level drawdown was accelerating. Charlie's **[14 October 1970 letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-10-14-combat-withdrawal/)** carries the line:

> *"Out of the field May 1, 1971 because all combat troops are being withdrawn."*

That date &mdash; 1 May 1971 &mdash; closely tracks the published Vietnamization schedule for the 3rd Brigade's combat operations standdown. Charlie wrote it about seven months in advance, signaling that the company-level understanding of the drawdown was already settled.

### Malaria — September 1970, Cam Ranh Bay

Charlie was hospitalized for **falciparum malaria in late September 1970**. His letters from this period &mdash; the [21 September "just diagnosed" letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-09-21-malaria-hospital/) and the [28 September Cam Ranh Bay convalescence letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1970-09-28-cam-ranh-bay/) on American National Red Cross stationery &mdash; place him at the rear-area hospital and convalescent center on the central coast. Malaria was a major cause of non-combat losses for 1-506th and the 101st as a whole in 1969-71.

### Congressional intervention — February 1971

In early February 1971 Charlie's mother [Peggy McMaster Eesley](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) wrote her congressman over what she perceived as an extension of Charlie's tour at **Camp Eagle**. Her [10 February 1971 letter to Terrie](/docs/peggy-to-terrie-1971-02-10/) was sent the same day as Charlie's own [10 February 1971 Da Nang letter](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-02-10-camp-eagle/). The two letters captured opposite poles of the same moment from inside the same family.

### One Hawaii R&R — April 1971

Charlie booked **one Hawaii R&R during his tour** &mdash; the trip in **April 1971**. The booking-and-conflict letters across the spring of 1971 &mdash; [29 March 1971](/docs/letters/charlie-to-terrie-1971-03-29-second-rr-booking/), [Dorothy Wildermuth's 26 March 1971 "utterly sick" objection](/docs/letters/dorothy-to-terrie-1971-03-26-utterly-sick/) &mdash; record the family's negotiation around the meet-up. (Earlier-pass dating that placed two R&Rs has been corrected: there was one Hawaii meet-up, April 1971.)

### ETS — 14 June 1971

Charlie's tour ended **14 June 1971**. The "X days left" countdowns in his late-tour letters all math back to that date. He went home, married Terrie that August, and the [1968-1971 correspondence](/docs/letters-from-vietnam/) closed.

## The unit went home — December 1971

The 1-506th's last combat operations were in May-June 1971. The unit stood down for the rest of the year, and the 3rd Brigade departed Vietnam in **early December 1971**, four years almost to the day after its 1967 arrival.

By that date roughly 50 men of Charlie Company had been killed in action across the four years; the wounded count was higher. The chronicle's purpose &mdash; per Higgins's own foreword &mdash; is to provide *"a memory aid to help recall the places and events that were etched into our lives many years ago"* for the surviving 1,200-plus veterans of the company who reunited four decades later.

## How to read the chronicle

The Higgins chronicle is organized by chapter, each covering a phase of the company's deployment:

1. Arrival (1967)
2. Song Be
3. Phuoc Vinh area
4. Dak To
5. Cu Chi then Head North (late 1968)
6. Camps Eagle, Evans, and FB Jack (late 1968 onward)
7. Road to the A Shau
8. A Shau Valley (Operation Apache Snow, May-June 1969 — Hamburger Hill)
9. DMZ Mission
10. Return to Camp Evans and FB Jack AO
11. Granite
12. Kathryn and Maureen
13. Ripcord and Kathryn (summer 1970)
14. Birmingham, Veghel, Tomahawk
15. Gladiator to Rakassan
16. Back to Rakkasan and the lowlands
17. Vandegrift and the lowlands
18. Gladiator and Barbara
19. Final days in the lowlands (1971)
20. Back to the "World" (the unit's return, December 1971)
21. Acronyms and Jargon

**Chapters 12 through 19 cover the operational window of Charlie Eesley's tour.** A reader working through Charlie's letters can find the matching chapters in Higgins's chronicle and read the unit-level events alongside the personal record.
