## What this collection is

Roberta Burnes Walker holds, in her own description from a July 2019 family email, *"a huge collection of mostly 1940s letters [that] may shed some light on this"* — *this* being the unresolved family-history question of how [Stella Sunn](/family/stella/) came to live with the Eesleys in Bexley, Ohio. The letters are written almost entirely in the hand of her grandfather [Charles Leonard Eesley](/family/charles-leonard-eesley/), the patriarch of the line through [Lillie Dale Chenoweth Eesley](/family/lillie-dale-chenoweth/). His handwriting, Roberta has said, is *"almost indecipherable."* The collection has not been read, transcribed, scanned, or digitized. Nothing from it appears on this site.

It is, at the same time, the single most consequential undeciphered source still to come for the Eesley side of this archive.

## Why the 1940s matter to this archive

The decade the collection spans is the decade the family's moral history bends on. The four arcs it almost certainly touches:

The Cabanatuan death. [Uncle Lyle Eesley](/family/lyle-eesley/) died at the Cabanatuan prisoner-of-war camp in the Philippines on 25 July 1942 — Charles Leonard's son and Will's brother. The wartime correspondence Charles Leonard kept with his scattered children would have moved through that event, before and after. None of what he wrote about losing Lyle has yet been read.

The Stella arrival. [Stella Sunn](/family/stella/) came to live with the Eesleys in Bexley within months of Lyle's death. The connection between the two — established in this archive only through Roberta's own 2019 reconstruction (*"she may have joined the scene after Lyle died"*) — would be documented in any letters Charles Leonard wrote about taking Stella in. The decision that turned what had been two separate chapters into one connected story is, on present knowledge, undocumented in primary sources.

The scattered children. Charles Leonard's eight children were grown by 1940. [Leonard "Len" Eesley](/family/leonard-david-eesley/) was in Plainwell, Michigan; [Donald Eesley](/family/don-eesley/) was in his own household; [Mary Eesley Bean](/family/mary-eesley-bean/) was building her register-keeping life; [Wilbur "Will" Eesley](/family/wilbur-eesley/) was establishing himself in central Ohio; [Helen Eesley Burnes](/family/helen-burnes/) was the youngest, just sixteen at the war's start. The wartime correspondence between Charles Leonard and his children would have moved across all of these households, in the years they were separated by war work, by school, by careers, by the war itself.

The Bexley years. Charles Leonard was at home in Bexley, with [Lillie Dale](/family/lillie-dale-chenoweth/), through the decade. The letters are the record of what he was thinking, hearing, and saying to his children from that kitchen.

## What is known about the physical collection

Almost nothing. Roberta's 2019 family email is the source of every claim this archive has been able to make about the collection. From that email:

- The collection is large (*"a huge collection"*).
- It is mostly from the 1940s.
- Most of it is in Charles Leonard's own hand.
- His handwriting is *"almost indecipherable."*
- It has not been read or transcribed.

What this archive does *not* know, on present evidence:

- The precise number of letters.
- The exact date range within the decade.
- The recipients — most likely Charles Leonard's adult children scattered across the country, but no individual letter has been confirmed.
- Whether the collection includes correspondence *received* by Charles Leonard (letters from his children to him) or only letters *written* by him that came back into the family's keeping after his death.
- Whether the collection includes any letters from or about Lyle in the months before Cabanatuan.
- Whether the collection includes any letters about Stella's arrival.

The undeciphered handwriting is the obstacle. Charles Leonard wrote a hand that even his own granddaughter, the keeper of the collection, cannot read at speed.

## The reading problem

A future reader of this collection would need either:

A trained paleographer or genealogist accustomed to early-twentieth-century cursive — the standard professional approach for handwritten correspondence of the 1930s–1950s, where the hand is legible but slow and the corpus is large.

Or a careful family-history pass — a reader who already knows the names, places, and events well enough to fill in around the indecipherable words, the way Robert Earl Wildermuth's transcribers did with his 1990 [Wildermuth/Fleming Heritage](/docs/) memoir.

Or a digitization-and-OCR pass — handwritten-text recognition has reached the point where letters of this era can be partially auto-transcribed, with the indecipherable runs flagged for human review.

None of these readings has happened. The single most likely next step is **a small pilot scan** — five to ten letters lifted from the collection and digitized at high resolution, so a reading pass can be attempted on a manageable sample before committing to the full collection.

## Status on this archive

Held by Roberta. Not yet on this site in any form. Referenced on the homepage and on Roberta's page as the most consequential source still to come, with no further claim made about its contents.

If at some point Roberta were willing to do a partial scan-and-transcribe pass — even of a handful of letters — the resulting documents would land here, each as its own letter entry under this parent collection, the way the Vietnam letters of the next generation are organized under [Letters from Vietnam](/docs/letters-from-vietnam/). The collection would then take its place as the Eesley-side equivalent of that Vietnam corpus, with the Cabanatuan and Stella threads as the load-bearing narratives the way Charlie's tour is the load-bearing narrative there.

The collection's existence is the thing this page records. The contents are still waiting.
