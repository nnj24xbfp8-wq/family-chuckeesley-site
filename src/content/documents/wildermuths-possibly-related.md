## Why this document exists

[Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/) spent **eighteen years** (1971–1989) researching the Wildermuth name across **Marietta census records, Philadelphia naturalization records, the LDS International Genealogical Index in Salt Lake City, the Orlando Public Library, and direct correspondence with German burgermeisters and parish offices in Württemberg**. The structured result of that work — Dale Eesley's [FamilySearch tree](/docs/dale-eesley-familysearch-tree/) — settled the **documented Wildermuth line** running back through:

> [Johann Michael Wildermuth](/family/johann-michael-wildermuth/) (b. 1830 Großaspach, the 1847 emigrant, naturalized Philadelphia 1853) → [William Clifford Wildermuth](/family/william-wildermuth/) (b. 1866 Marietta) → [Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/) (b. 1899 Marietta) → [Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/) (b. 1924 Marietta) → Terrie → Chuck.

Robert Earl also traced the line backward into Württemberg through [Andreus Wildermuth](/family/andreus-wildermuth/) the vineyard gardener (b. ~1748 Rielingshausen) and into the 17th century via [Johannes Wildermuth](/family/johannes-wildermuth-1650/) (b. 1650).

But the **Wildermuth surname is not unique** to Robert Earl's line. The name appears in multiple Württemberg villages of the 17th–19th centuries; multiple emigration waves brought separate Wildermuth families to America independently. Other Wildermuth-surname families with their own American descendants exist, may have crossed paths with Robert Earl during his research correspondence, and may even share an undocumented common ancestor with him deep in Württemberg.

**This document tracks the Wildermuth-surname people in the archive who are not yet linked to Robert Earl's documented line** — keeping the question open rather than forcing a premature resolution.

## The Jacob and Elizabeth Wildermuth family (c. 1885–1895)

The principal case: a **formal sepia studio cabinet-card portrait** of **eleven figures** held in the family archive. Chuck Eesley identified the cast in 2026 as:

> *"Wildermuth Family. Back Men — LtoR — Charles E, John 'Leb', Harry J, Jesse W. Women Middle LtoR — Maude B, Lucy, Zella M, 'Lanessa' Louise. Jacob and Elizabeth sitting. Troy B middle."*

The patriarch **[Jacob Wildermuth](/family/jacob-wildermuth/)** sits front-center; his wife **[Elizabeth Wildermuth](/family/elizabeth-wildermuth-jacob-wife/)** sits beside him; their small son **Troy B** is held between them. Their **eight other adult children** stand around them: Charles E, John "Leb", Harry J, and Jesse W (the four sons in the back row); Maude B, Lucy, Zella M, and "Lanessa" Louise (the four daughters in the middle row).

**None of these nine given names appears in Dale Eesley's FamilySearch tree.** The structured record does not connect Jacob Wildermuth's family to Robert Earl's documented line. Three readings remain plausible:

1. **A genuinely separate Wildermuth-surname family** of German-American immigrants from a different Württemberg village or a different German Wildermuth-bearing region (Hessen, the Pfalz, Baden), with their own separate emigration to America independent of Johann Michael's 1847 voyage.
2. **A distant-cousin branch** sharing a deep common Württemberg ancestor with Robert Earl's line — possibly several generations back at one of the Rielingshausen or Großaspach common ancestors — but with the bridging documentary record never reconstructed.
3. **A photograph Robert Earl collected during his 1971–1989 genealogy research** — exchanged with a fellow Wildermuth-surname genealogist whose own research had reached this family, retained in his archive as suggestive material, never resolved.

The c. 1885–1895 dating (cabinet card style, women's clothing, Jacob's apparent age) suggests Jacob himself was born **c. 1835–1845** — the same generation as [Catharina Boeshar Wildermuth](/family/catharina-boeshar-wildermuth/) (b. 1840), Robert Earl's actual great-grandmother. If Jacob and Robert Earl's lines do share a common ancestor, the bridge would be a generation or two further back, in Germany.

## What "Leb" might mean

The "John 'Leb'" identification on the back-row brother carries an open philological question. **"Leb"** as an English-context nickname most plausibly derives from one of:

- **Lebrecht** — a German masculine given name (literally "live rightly"), used in 18th–19th-century Württemberg and Hessen families with Pietist religious traditions.
- **Lebanon** — used as a U.S. masculine given name in some 19th-century rural Protestant American families, drawn from the biblical place name.
- **Lev** or **Levi** — Hebrew-derived names occasionally Anglicized as "Leb" in some American family traditions.

The Lebrecht reading is the most consistent with the Wildermuth-as-Württemberg-German pattern and would suggest the family carried the German given-name tradition into America longer than Robert Earl's branch did.

## Other Wildermuth-surname people in the archive

Beyond the Jacob and Elizabeth family, several Wildermuth-surname people appear in Dale Eesley's GEDCOM tree (in addition to the documented Robert Earl line) — distant 17th–19th century Württemberg Wildermuths whose precise relationship to Robert Earl is structured but not narratively explored in this archive:

- **Johann Jacob Wildermuth**, **Johann Christian Wildermuth**, **Johann Georg Wildermuth**, **Andreas Adam Wildermuth**, **Gottlieb Wildermuth**, **Johann Adam Wildermuth**, **Daniel Wildermuth**, **Friderika Magdalena Wildermuth**, **Carolina Wildermuth** — all Württemberg Wildermuths of various 18th–19th-century generations, documented in the structured record but not yet narratively positioned.
- **Jacob Friedrich Wildermuth** (b. 18 July 1770 Rielingshausen, d. 7 May 1771 Rielingshausen, age 10 months) — a documented infant death in Robert Earl's own deep Wildermuth ancestral chain. Likely a sibling or cousin in the Rielingshausen generation just before Andreus the *Gartner*.
- **Catherina Dorothea Wildermuth**, **Katherine Debona Wildermuth** — Wildermuth-surname women appearing in the GEDCOM, relationship to Robert Earl's line not yet narratively positioned.

These Württemberg Wildermuths are *probably related* to Robert Earl through the documented Rielingshausen-and-Großaspach ancestor chain — but the narrative connection isn't yet built. They sit closer to "documented but not yet portraited" than to the truly-open Jacob-and-Elizabeth case.

## Research follow-up questions

For someone able to take this thread further, the productive next steps would be:

1. **Search U.S. census records** for a **Jacob Wildermuth with wife Elizabeth and nine children** in the 1880, 1890, or 1900 census. The unusual names Troy B and Zella M would narrow the search considerably. Most likely states: Pennsylvania, Ohio, West Virginia, Michigan, Indiana, Wisconsin, or one of the other midwestern states with German-immigrant Wildermuth concentrations.
2. **Cross-reference Robert Earl's 1971–1989 research correspondence** — if any of his letters surviving in the family archive name a Wildermuth-surname research contact, that contact might be a member of Jacob's family or a descendant of one of Jacob's children.
3. **Search Württemberg parish registers** for a **Jacob Wildermuth born c. 1835–1845** in any village within the broader Wildermuth-surname region. The Rielingshausen-Großaspach-Marbach-Pleidelsheim cluster would be the first place to look; the broader Backnang district the second.
4. **Photographic facial-comparison** — when the scan of the Jacob and Elizabeth portrait is digitized in this archive, comparing the Wildermuth-side faces (Jacob, the four adult sons) against [William Clifford Wildermuth](/family/william-wildermuth/) (1866) and [Edward Frederick Wildermuth](/family/edward-frederick-wildermuth/) (1873) in the family photographs could give a family-resemblance signal for or against a close-cousin reading.
