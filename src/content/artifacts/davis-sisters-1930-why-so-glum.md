The second of the Davis-sisters frames in the 1990 Heritage — Mary, Dorothy, Betty at six, five, and four, holding hands in front of a wooden fence. The typed caption Robert Earl added beneath the print carries the joke the photograph poses: *"From the left: Mary Davis - age 6; Dorothy Davis - age 5 and Betty Davis - age 4. Why so glum?"* All three are looking at the camera with the same flat unsmiling expression — a small documentary in 1930 child-portraiture, where holding still for the exposure mattered more than smiling for it.

## What it documents

It is one of a pair Robert Earl reproduced on the same page of the Heritage with the lighter [puppies-and-bare-feet frame](/archive/davis-sisters-puppies-1931/) immediately below it; the two are deliberately matched to show the same three girls a year apart, in different moods. The pair is the Heritage's central piece of evidence that the Davis sisters did grow up in one another's daily company through the late-1920s and early-1930s Ohio childhood that the rest of the section narrates.

The "why so glum?" sister set is the same three children whose surnames would later become *Wildermuth, Wolfe,* and Mary's married name (TBD in the archive) — the [Wildermuth-Wolfe-Davis kinship cluster](/archive/betty-norm-wolfe-wedding-1945/) that runs through three generations of Chuck's childhood reminiscences. The three faces in the 1930 frame are the founding-image of that cluster.
