**Close-up dress-uniform portrait of Robert Earl Wildermuth as a U.S. Air Force officer** — the post-recall portrait from the early years of his Air Force career, c. 1951–1955. Identified by Chuck Eesley 2026.

## What the photograph records

Robert Earl wears the **U.S. Air Force dress uniform** with a garrison cap (gold officer's insignia visible at the front), the **"US"** collar device on his left lapel, **shoulder rank** clearly visible, and the top of wings visible at his chest. He smiles broadly at the camera — the same broad smile that appears on his [1946 wedding day portrait](/archive/robert-earl-dorothy-wedding-1946/) and that his daughter Terrie would later carry into her own.

The uniform-type (U.S. Air Force, not Army Air Corps — the Air Force became a separate service in **September 1947**) dates the photograph to **post-1947**. The early-30s apparent age fits the **1951 recall** through the **mid-1950s** range. The cleanness of the uniform and the formal close-up framing suggest this is a **commissioned-portrait** style — either the post-recall portrait from June 1951 (when he was sworn in at Hamilton AFB as a First Lieutenant), or an early Captain-rank portrait from his Korea/Florida/Tampa years.

## In the memoir's frame

In **May 1951** — per his [memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/) — Robert Earl was recalled from his Hamilton-AFB sergeant clerk-typist assignment to active commissioned duty as a **First Lieutenant**, taking ten days' leave to buy proper officer uniforms and insignia. Within months he had completed bombardier training at Mather, low-level night training at Langley, and survival school at Stead, AFB, Reno; the deployment to Korea was interrupted by the **serious automobile accident at Camp Stoneman** that put him in the hospital at Parks AFB for ten months with a fractured skull and left facial paralysis. He eventually went to Korea on the ground, commanding a communications squadron and being promoted to **Captain**.

This portrait most likely dates to the **immediate post-recall, pre-deployment period — early-to-mid 1951** — when the dress uniform was new and the smile of a returned and re-commissioned officer was on him.

The four together cover his uniformed life from young 1st Lt through Lieutenant Colonel.
