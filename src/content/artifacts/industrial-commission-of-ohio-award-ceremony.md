**Industrial Commission of Ohio Special Award ceremony**, c. 1940s–1960s. **The recipient is [Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/)** — Robert Earl Wildermuth's machinist father — at his Marietta-area manufacturing workplace. **Confirmed by Chuck Eesley, 2026.**

## What the photograph shows

**Three men inside a factory office or supply room.** Behind them are wood-paneled supply cabinets stocked with boxes of fittings (visible boxes include "Multigraph" and other industrial supplies). A small lamp hangs from the ceiling above; a typewriter is partly visible at the right edge.

- **At left**: An **older worker in overalls and a peaked cap** — late middle-aged, with glasses, work clothes — looking at the camera. Possibly Earl Adam Wildermuth himself or the workplace's senior man.
- **In the middle**: A **second worker in overalls and a cap**, looking down at the certificate, with a faint smile. He holds the Industrial Commission of Ohio "Special Award" certificate at center.
- **At right**: A **man in tie and white shirt** — clearly **management** rather than shop floor, smartly dressed, mid-handshake with the older worker on the left.

The certificate visible reads *"The Industrial Commission of Ohio / Special Award"* with finer print below not legible in the scan.

The Industrial Commission of Ohio is the **state's workers' compensation agency**, established 1913 and active through the 20th century. The Commission issued **"Special Awards" for workplace safety achievements** — typically given to companies or to individual workers who completed a documented period without lost-time injuries. The handshake-and-certificate composition is consistent with a workplace-safety-milestone ceremony of the era.

## The group portrait

A second frame in the archive shows **the full group of co-workers** at the same award ceremony — about ten men in a factory setting, with **industrial machinery** (pulleys, flywheels, machine-tool equipment) visible to either side, posed in two rows. The man holding the certificate at center-front of that frame is the same one from the handshake photo. The setting is a small-to-medium factory floor of mid-century industrial Ohio — concrete floor, fluorescent lighting, the equipment of a machine shop.

## On the family memory layer

Robert Earl's [memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/) records his father Earl Adam as having *"started his apprenticeship for his chosen life's work"* as a machinist after returning from the family's North Carolina cotton-mill year in the mid-1920s. He worked at a Marietta-area concrete-silo-stave manufacturing plant for many years (Robert Earl summered there as the company's water boy at age twelve). **Earl Adam's machinist career is the period this award ceremony fits into** — a Marietta-area industrial workplace, a worker of his generation being recognized for safety or service milestone, a clean shop floor and a small white-collar manager doing the handshake.

**This photograph is the only known visual record of Earl Adam's workplace life** — the man who built the family economy his son Robert Earl built the Stanford-and-Air-Force career on, and the man whose 17 January 1972 letter to Robert Earl became the founding document of the eighteen-year Wildermuth/Fleming Heritage genealogical project.
