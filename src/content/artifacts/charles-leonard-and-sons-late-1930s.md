Six men in formal dark suits against a hedge, with one young grandson in shorts and a light sweater at the patriarch's right hand. From left to right, the figures are:

1. **[Leonard David Eesley](/family/leonard-david-eesley/)** (b. 1904) — the eldest son, in his early thirties at the time of this frame.
2. **[Lyle Eesley](/family/lyle-eesley/)** (1914–1942) — younger, fair-haired; he would die at Cabanatuan POW Camp on Luzon in July 1942, three to five years after this photograph.
3. **[Donald Stuart "Don" Eesley](/family/don-eesley/)** (1908–1975) — middle.
4. **[Will Eesley](/family/wilbur-eesley/)** (1910–1986) — Chuck's grandfather, the architect-to-be.
5. **[Dale Dudley George Eesley](/family/dale-eesley/)** (1916–1939) — the youngest of the five in the frame; he would die at twenty-two in **July 1939**, which marks the latest possible date for this photograph.

At the far right, the elder man in the lighter suit is **[Charles Leonard Eesley](/family/charles-leonard-eesley/)** the patriarch (1879–1972). The young boy standing in front of him is **[Tommy Eesley](/family/tommy-eesley/)** — Leonard David's son, Charles Leonard's eldest grandson, and the "nephew Tommy" of [Aunt Maggie's deck slide of Will at Black Lake](/family/wilbur-eesley/) around 1933.

## The date window

The presence of **Dale Dudley George** (d. 12 July 1939) bounds this photograph **before July 1939**; the absence of Charles Leonard and Lillie Dale's other two sons **Thomas Leonard (b. 1906)** and **James Michael "Mike" (b. 1908)** is unexplained but doesn't affect the date logic. Tommy's age in the frame &mdash; roughly nine to twelve &mdash; places it most likely in **1937–1939**, putting Lyle in his early-to-mid twenties and Dale around the same.

## Why this frame matters

This is the only photograph of **Lyle Eesley** placed in this archive: a young man, about twenty-three or twenty-four, second from left, the year or two before he shipped to the Philippines and never came home. The frame catches him at home in Bexley, in formal dress with his father and brothers, in the last possible window before the war redirected the rest of his life.

It is also one of two surviving photographs of Tommy as a small boy in this archive's keeping (the other being the c. 1933 Black Lake frame with his uncle Will).
