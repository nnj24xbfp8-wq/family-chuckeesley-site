**Banbury** sits in northern Oxfordshire, about four miles east of **Hanwell** — the parish from which the volunteer at the Oxford public library worked back the Eesley line to **Joseph Eesley** and **Frances Ayris** in the early 18th century. For the Eesleys at Hanwell, Banbury would have been the obvious market town: where you took grain, sold flour, bought what the household couldn't grow, attended the wider parish and county business. By the time **John Eesley** (b. 1800) moved the family across the county line into [Old Stratford](/places/old-stratford-rother-street/) some time before the 1851 census, Banbury had been the family's center of trade for generations.

## The frames Chuck and Lijie made

On the 2018 trip the two of them did the photographic walk through Banbury that the present-day family had not yet done:

- The famous **Banbury Cross** — the Victorian replacement (1859) for the original medieval and 17th-century crosses long demolished — with the equestrian *Fine Lady upon a White Horse* statue (added 2005) directly beside it. The cross is the rhyme: *"Ride a cock-horse to Banbury Cross / To see a fine lady upon a white horse."* Three of Chuck and Lijie's frames are of the cross and the statue.
- **St Mary's Banbury** — the early-19th-century parish church on Horse Fair, with its distinctive cylindrical Georgian tower; rebuilt 1790–1797 to a design by S. P. Cockerell after the medieval church was demolished. Three of the frames are of the church tower and the porch.
- The **"A Brief History of the Church House"** historical sign on the Banbury Civic Society's plaque, which Chuck framed as evidence of the same family research context the Old Stratford photograph was made in.
- A frame at the **People's Park** signpost in the town center.
