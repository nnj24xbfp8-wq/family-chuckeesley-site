**413 Harmar Street, Marietta, Washington County, Ohio** — the **Wildermuth family house** of [Earl Adam Wildermuth](/family/earl-a-wildermuth/), [Sadye Irene (Fleming) Wildermuth](/family/sadye-fleming-wildermuth/), and their five children, including [Robert Earl Wildermuth](/family/robert-earl-wildermuth/) (Chuck's maternal grandfather). The address is **the central physical anchor of Robert Earl's childhood** and the venue of some of the most vivid scenes in his [1989 memoir](/docs/robert-earl-wildermuth-memoir/).

## The house Robert Earl describes

In Robert Earl's own words:

> *"About 1929 my mother and father were successful in purchasing a house that was always their dream house at 413 Harmar Street. This was a three bedroom house with a large dining room and a fair sized living room and kitchen. It sat upon a brick foundation approximately four feet high and was located on a steady rise three blocks from the Muskingum River."*

The Wildermuth family **lived there over fourteen years** — from about 1929 through the early 1940s, when Robert Earl shipped to the war. **All four of Robert Earl's younger siblings** (Carl Edward, Norma Jean, Betty Joan, and his oldest sister Ruth Irene was already born by then) were either born or raised at this address. The dining room — large enough to hold "all of the furniture out" when his parents pushed it aside for neighborhood-children Christmas parties — was the household's emotional center.

## The Marietta neighborhood

**Harmar Street is on the West Side of Marietta**, in the section of the town historically called **Harmar** — the original 1787 settlement around Fort Harmar at the junction of the Muskingum and Ohio rivers. The street runs roughly parallel to the Muskingum River, three blocks back from the bank, on a "steady rise" toward the higher ground of the West Side. The neighborhood was the small-town, working-class West Side counterpart to the established Marietta business district across the Muskingum bridge, and it was a German-immigrant-descended neighborhood — Schlichers, Wildermuths, and the other 19th-century Pfalzer and Württemberger families' descendants formed a substantial portion of the residents.

Robert Earl attended **Harmar Elementary School** six blocks from home, built on the high point of land at the river junction where the original 1787 Fort Harmar had stood; then **Marietta Junior High** and **Marietta High School** across the Muskingum bridge in the main business district. The bicycle his father had let him earn through a Baltimore summer of work (1936 or 1937) made that cross-town commute possible — fifteen minutes each way through the synchronized traffic lights of downtown Marietta.

## The 1937 flood story

The house — being three blocks from the Muskingum, at four feet on its brick foundation — survived but **flooded badly during the [Marietta flood of 1937](/docs/marietta-1937-flood/)**, when the Ohio River reached its highest crest in recorded history. The flood scene is the most vivid disaster moment in Robert Earl's memoir; it has its own document page in this archive.

## What the address meant

The 1929 purchase was the family's deliberate economic step up — from the small West Side rental houses of Earl Adam's apprentice-machinist years into "their dream house." That a German-immigrant-shoemaker's grandson and a Fleming-line working-class father could buy a substantial three-bedroom house with a four-foot brick foundation in 1929 — at the bottom of the Depression — speaks to Earl Adam's steady factory wages and the family's frugality. It is the maternal-side equivalent of the paternal Eesley family's [Seventh Street house](/family/margaret-mcmaster-eesley/) and the later [Highland Ridge Road house](/family/wilbur-eesley/) on the Eesley side: the address where the children's adult identities formed.

The house outlasted Earl Adam and Sadye. By the time Sadye died in 1976 and Earl Adam followed in 1977, the family had moved to **320 Harmar Street** — a few addresses up the same street — where Robert Earl's blind brother Carl was living with their father in old age.

## Photographs and current state

No photograph of the house at 413 Harmar Street is yet in this archive. **A photographic record of the current state of the address — pilgrimage photography in the [Rielingshausen / Grossaspach](/places/rielingshausen-church/) tradition** — would be a natural next-step contribution from a Marietta-visiting family member. The house may still stand; the West Side of Marietta has retained much of its early-20th-century building stock.
