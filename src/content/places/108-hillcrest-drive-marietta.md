108 Hillcrest Drive, Marietta, Ohio is the house [Betty Jean (Davis) Wolfe](/family/betty-davis/) and [Norman "Norm" Graham Wolfe](/family/norman-graham-wolfe/) lived in across most of their adult lives — the holiday-host house Chuck Eesley remembers as one of the two anchors of his childhood holiday-meal life, alongside [Grandma Peggy's 424 N 7th Street house](/places/424-n-seventh-street-marietta/) on the paternal Eesley side. Chuck's eulogy for his father lists the places that should hold his father's name longest; Hillcrest does not appear in that list because it was Chuck's mother's side. But for Chuck himself — the small boy at the holiday table, the sick child on the couch, the older boy at Thanksgiving — the Hillcrest house holds a piece of his name too.

## What the house was

Betty and Norm married in 1945, days before Norm shipped to Germany with the Army. They settled in Marietta when he came back from the war and stayed there for sixty-eight of their next sixty-nine married years — moving only in 2013, when both were nearly 90, to Largo, Florida, to be near two of their three daughters. The Hillcrest house was their home through that whole Marietta arc. Their daughters [Victoria Ann](/family/victoria-ann-wolfe-schwartz/), [Norma Jean](/family/norma-jean-wolfe-grundset/), and [Peggy Sue](/family/peggy-sue-wolfe/) were raised there. The 1960s family-of-four-at-the-fireplace photograph in this place page documents the kind of frame the household generated decade by decade.

The architecture and street profile of the house is open research — the surviving photographs are interior frames from across the decades, with the brick fireplace, the wood-paneled walls, the built-in bookshelves, and the kitchen-and-dining area facing windows out the back as the recurring elements. The visit-images gallery above runs across roughly fifty years of the same room.

## The holiday-table pattern Chuck remembers

Chuck's family-memory account, transmitted June 2026: *"Easter, Thanksgiving, and Christmas were often spent at their house."* Across the three-decade window of Chuck's childhood through young adulthood — c. 1982 through the early 2010s — the Hillcrest holiday table set three times a year for the Eesley family. Two generations of Davis women hosting their sister's grandchild's family at the table is the kind of three-generation arrangement that holds extended families together across the period institutional family-care patterns shrink in the United States. Betty's older sister Dorothy Davis was Chuck's grandmother on the maternal Wildermuth-Davis side. The family-care arc of three Davis-line generations sitting at the Hillcrest table is what this place page exists to record.

Betty's 2015 obituary, written by her three daughters, framed her hospitality directly:

> *"She was an excellent cook and baker who loved to have people come for a meal. Everything was delicious."*

Chuck's memory of the Hillcrest holiday dinners — in two sentences his great-aunt's own family wrote. Every Easter, Thanksgiving, and Christmas table set at 108 Hillcrest where Chuck and his parents sat down was *"everything was delicious."* The [2011 Thanksgiving photograph](/archive/norm-betty-wolfe-thanksgiving-2011/) above is the documentary frame of that exact pattern, three years before Norm and Betty both died.

## The sick-day caregiver layer

When Chuck had to stay home from school as a kid, he was often either at the Hillcrest house or at his godmother [Faye Angel](/family/faye-angel/)'s house. The two of them — Betty Davis Wolfe and Faye Angel — were the two sick-day caregivers of his Marietta childhood. The two households together formed an extended-family-care network around young Chuck the way small-town extended families used to before institutional daycare was the default. The [c. 1985–1990 photograph of young Chuck on the Hillcrest couch with what appears to have been a sprained ankle](/archive/young-chuck-with-cast-at-hillcrest/) is the surviving documentary frame of that arrangement.

That a great-aunt was watching over her sister's grandson's son through his bad-health days at her Hillcrest house — a great-grand-nephew, three generations down — is the kind of three-generation family-care arrangement the GEDCOM dates don't capture but the family memory does. The pattern that ran through Betty's whole life — nursing her dying mother through her teens-and-early-twenties, raising her three daughters, hosting holiday tables for her sister's grandchildren, and helping her Sixth Street Church of Christ congregation in their losses and illnesses — runs through this house specifically.

## The Sixth Street Church of Christ care-network node

Betty's 2015 obituary names the wider care arc that the Hillcrest house was a node in:

> *"Betty and Norm were always ready and willing to help those in need. They were happy to take food to friends and members of their congregation, the Sixth Street Church of Christ, when any family suffered loss or sickness; to visit folks in the hospital or nursing home; and to drive them to appointments or church services."*

Across decades, a quiet practical-care kind of Christianity ran out of this kitchen — meals for the sick assembled here and driven elsewhere, hospital visits planned from here, rides to appointments organized here. Chuck's childhood sick-day visits at Hillcrest were one node of a much larger care-and-meal network the Wolfes ran across the Marietta congregation. The Eesley family was one set of people among many they fed and visited and drove and showed up for from this house.

## The Marietta-address geography

108 Hillcrest sits in the small cluster of Marietta-area family addresses across the maternal and paternal sides of Chuck's family:

- **[Highland Ridge Road](/places/highland-ridge-road-marietta/)** — Chuck's father's side: Will the architect's designed-and-built house, where Charlie raised Chuck.
- **[651 Sixth Street](/places/651-sixth-street-marietta/)** — Will and Peggy's first Marietta house, where Charlie grew up.
- **[424 N 7th Street](/places/424-n-seventh-street-marietta/)** — Grandma Peggy's widow's house, after Will's June 1986 death; the after-school house of Chuck's later childhood.
- **[413 Harmar Street](/places/413-harmar-street-marietta/)** — Chuck's mother's side: Robert Earl Wildermuth's childhood house, three blocks up from the Muskingum.
- **108 Hillcrest Drive** (this page) — Chuck's mother's side: the Wolfes' house, the holiday-host and sick-day-caregiver anchor.

Five small Ohio-river-town addresses inside walking distance of each other, holding most of the family geography of Chuck's first decades.
